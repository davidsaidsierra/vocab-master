## Section 001 — Question Words

**Common question words and what they ask about:**

- **Who** (person) — A: Who's that man? B: That's Peter.
- **Where** (place) — A: Where do you live? B: In London.
- **When** (time) — A: When did he arrive? B: In the afternoon.
- **Why** (reason) — A: Why did you leave? B: Because I was tired.
- **What** (thing) — A: What do you need? B: Your car.
- **What time** (specific time) — A: What time did he call? B: At seven.
- **What kind of** (type) — A: What kind of car is it? B: A hybrid.
- **How many** (countable quantity) — A: How many beds are there? B: Three.
- **How much** (uncountable quantity) — A: How much tea is there? B: A lot.
- **How long** (duration) — A: How long did you stay? B: Two weeks.
- **How often** (frequency) — A: How often do you go? B: Every day.
- **Which** (choice) — A: Which car is better? B: The red one.
- **How far** (distance) — A: How far is the town? B: One mile away.
- **Whose** (possession) — A: Whose book is this? B: It's mine.
- **How old** (age) — A: How old is your sister? B: She's 27.
- **How** (manner) — A: How did you go? B: By car.

## Section 002 — Word Order in Questions

**Questions with BE:** Question word + BE + subject + complement?
- Is Sharon from the US?
- How old are you?
- Where were you yesterday?
- When was she born?

**Questions with DO/DOES/DID:** Question word + auxiliary + subject + infinitive?
- What time does he have lunch?
- What car do you have?

## Section 003 — Present Simple: Form

**Positive:** Subject + verb (add -s for he/she/it).
- I/You/We/They work.
- He/She/It works.

**Negative:** Subject + don't/doesn't + infinitive.
- I do not work / I don't work.
- He does not work / He doesn't work.

**Question:** Do/Does + subject + infinitive?
- Do you work?
- Does she work? (NOT: Does she works?)

**Short answers:** Yes, I do. / No, she doesn't.

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ He doesn't works. → ✅ He doesn't work.
- ❌ Does she works? → ✅ Does she work?

## Section 004 — Different Uses of BE

**Introducing people:** I'm Steven and this is Isabella.
**Age:** She is twenty / She is twenty years old.
**Weather:** It's sunny today. It is very cold this morning.
**Nationality:** Liam is from Ireland, and Sonia is from Spain.
**Feelings:** She is very tired.
**Colour:** Our dog is black. His eyes are blue.
**Prices:** It's seven pounds. This T-shirt is twenty dollars.
**Time:** What time is it? It's ten past four.
**Descriptions:** He is very short and thin. These cars are old and cheap.

## Section 005 — Present Simple of BE

**Positive:** I am (I'm), You are (You're), He is (He's), She is (She's), It is (It's), We are (We're), They are (They're).

**Negative:** I am not (I'm not), You are not (You're not / You aren't), He is not (He's not / He isn't), etc.

**Question:** Am I? Are you? Is he? Is she? Is it? Are we? Are they?

**Short answers:**
- ✅ Yes, I am. / No, I'm not.
- ✅ Yes, she is. / No, she isn't.

**Common mistake:** We CANNOT use short forms in positive short answers.
- ❌ A: Are you happy? B: Yes, I'm.
- ✅ A: Are you happy? B: Yes, I am.

## Section 006 — This, That, These, Those

- **This** = singular, near. → This is a monster.
- **That** = singular, far. → That is a monster.
- **These** = plural, near. → These are monsters.
- **Those** = plural, far. → Those are monsters.

## Section 008 — A / AN & Plurals

**A vs AN:**
- **A** + consonant sound: a table, a box.
- **AN** + vowel sound: an orange, an egg.
- **AN + silent H-:** an hour.
- **A + pronounced [ju:]:** a university, a European.
- **AN + U- pronounced [ʌ]:** an umbrella.

**Regular plural spelling:**
- General rule → add -s: student → students.
- After -s, -ch, -sh, -x → add -es: bus → buses, box → boxes.
- Consonant + -y → delete -y, add -ies: baby → babies, country → countries.
- After -ay, -ey, -oy → add -s: day → days, toy → toys.
- After -f or -fe → delete and add -ves: shelf → shelves, wife → wives.

**Irregular plurals:** man → men, woman → women, child → children, foot → feet, tooth → teeth, fish → fish, mouse → mice, sheep → sheep.

**Common mistake:** Do not use a/an with plurals.
- ❌ These are a tables. → ✅ These are tables.

## Section 009 — Adjectives in English

**Adjective + noun:** This is an interesting book.

**am/is/are + adjective:** This book is interesting. Her eyes are blue. Is this food Chinese?

**feel/look/smell/sound/taste + adjective:**
- Your idea sounds interesting.
- This soup tastes horrible.
- These cookies smell delicious.
- I feel bad.

**Adjectives do NOT have a plural form.**
- ❌ These are my favourites shoes. → ✅ These are my favourite shoes.
- ❌ She has blues eyes. → ✅ She has blue eyes.

## Section 012 — Adverbs of Frequency

**Frequency scale:**
- 100% always
- usually
- often
- sometimes
- rarely / hardly ever
- 0% never

**Word order:**
- **Positive:** Subject + frequency adverb + verb. → He often cooks pasta.
- **With BE:** BE + frequency adverb. → They are never at home when we call.
- **Question:** Subject + (auxiliary) + frequency adverb + verb? → Does he often cook? / Are you always late?

**Common mistake:** Use NEVER and HARDLY EVER with positive verbs (they are already negative).
- ❌ He isn't never late. → ✅ He is never late.
- ❌ They don't hardly ever go to the library. → ✅ They hardly ever go to the library.

## Section 014 — Possessive 's — Use & Form

**Possession / Relationship:**
- Singular noun + 's: My father's car, Mary's computer, Peter's brother. (NOT the computer of Mary.)

**Shops and houses:**
- I'm at John's (= at John's house).
- I'm going to the baker's (= the baker's shop).

**Of-construction (for things):**
- the end of the street, a picture of the sea, the secret of life.

**Compound nouns (things often used together):**
- the city center (= the center of the city), the car keys (= the keys of the car).

**Plural rules:**
- Irregular plural not ending in -s + 's: children's story, men's room.
- Regular plural ending in -s + ': the boys' restroom, the teachers' lounge.
- Singular noun ending in -s + 's or +': Chris's (or Chris') computer.

**Shared vs separate possession:**
- Anna and Tom's car = they share one car.
- Anna's and Tom's cars = each has one.

## Section 015 — Prepositions of Place: AT / IN / ON

**AT (a point):**
- at the door, at the crossroads, at the traffic light
- at the top of the stairs, at the bottom of the page, at the end of the street
- group activities: at a party, at a concert, at the cinema
- school/work/home: at school, at university, at home, at work
- shops: at the bakery, at a café, at the chemist's

**IN (a 3D space or territory):**
- in the bag, in the classroom, in a box
- in London, in Europe, in the Alps, in the world
- vehicles: in the car, in a van
- water: in the sea, in a river, in a lake, in the swimming pool

**ON (a surface or line):**
- on the table, on the carpet, on the roof, on the sofa, on the wall
- on the fifth floor, on the right, on the left
- public transport: on the bus, on the train, on a plane
- media: on the radio, on TV, on the Internet, on a website

## Section 016 — Can / Can't — Form & Use

**Positive:** Subject + can + infinitive. → Laura can sing very well.
**Negative:** Subject + cannot / can't + infinitive. → I can't speak German.
**Question:** Can + subject + infinitive? → Can I sit here?
**Short answer:** Yes, I can. / No, I can't.

**Uses:**
- Ability: She can swim.
- Permission: You can't park your car here.
- Possibility: I can't come to the party.
- Requests / orders: Can you drive me to the station? Can I have a soda, please?

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ She cans swim. → ✅ She can swim.
- ❌ Do you can speak English? → ✅ Can you speak English?
- ❌ I can't to come. → ✅ I can't come.

## Section 017 — Present Continuous: Form

**Positive:** Subject + am/is/are + verb-ing. → I'm working.
**Negative:** Subject + am/is/are + not + verb-ing. → He isn't working.
**Question:** Am/Is/Are + subject + verb-ing? → Are you working?
**Short answer:** Yes, I am. / No, I'm not.

**-ing spelling:**
- General rule: verb + -ing (work → working).
- Consonant + -e: delete -e, add -ing (write → writing).
- Consonant + vowel + consonant (stressed): double the consonant + -ing (swim → swimming, run → running).
- After -ie: delete -ie, add -ying (die → dying, lie → lying).

## Section 018 — The Imperative

**Form:** infinitive without "to" (no subject).
**Negative:** Do not / Don't + infinitive.

**Uses:**
- **Orders:** Stop talking! Fasten your seatbelt.
- **Instructions:** Press the green button. Open the package carefully.
- **Warnings:** Don't touch that cable! Stop the car!
- **Invitations:** Come and sit with us. Have some cookies.
- **Offers / advice:** Get some sleep. Tell her how you feel.
- **Prohibitions:** Do not disturb. Don't shout.

## Section 019 — Was / Were: Form

**Positive:** I/He/She/It was. You/We/They were.
**Negative:** wasn't / weren't. → He wasn't tired.
**Question:** Was/Were + subject? → Was she at home?
**Short answer:** Yes, she was. / No, she wasn't.

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ He didn't was. → ✅ He wasn't.
- ❌ Did she was? → ✅ Was she?

## Section 021 — Present Simple vs Present Continuous: Form

**Present Simple:**
- Positive: I/you/we/they work. He/she/it works.
- Negative: I don't work. He doesn't work.
- Question: Do you work? Does he work?
- Short answer: Yes, I do. / No, he doesn't.

**Present Continuous:**
- Positive: I am working. You/we/they are working. He/she/it is working.
- Negative: I'm not working. He isn't working.
- Question: Am I working? Are you working? Is he working?
- Short answer: Yes, I am. / No, he isn't.

## Section 025 — Basic Word Order

**Subject + verb + object (+ place + time):**
- I play the guitar.
- I will be in my office at 3 pm.

**Adverbs of frequency** go before the main verb but after BE:
- He often cooks pasta. / Are you always late?

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ I like this music. Is nice! → ✅ I like this music. **It** is nice! (Don't forget the subject.)
- ❌ I like very much pizza. → ✅ I like pizza very much. (Don't separate verb and object.)
- ❌ I will be at 3 pm in my office. → ✅ I will be in my office at 3 pm. (Place before time.)

## Section 026 — Have Got: Form

**Positive:** I/you/we/they have got (I've got). He/she/it has got (He's got).
- I've got a car. / He's got a car.

**Negative:** haven't got / hasn't got.
- I haven't got a car. / She hasn't got a sister.

**Question:** Have/Has + subject + got?
- Have you got a car? / Has she got time?

**Short answer:** Yes, I have. / No, she hasn't.

## Section 031 — Comparative & Superlative Adjectives (overview)

**Comparative:**
- 1 syllable → add -er: tall → taller.
- 2 or more syllables → more + adjective: interesting → more interesting.

**Superlative:**
- 1 syllable → the + -est: tall → the tallest.
- 2 or more syllables → the most + adjective: interesting → the most interesting.

## Section 035 — A, THE, Zero Article

**A / AN** (indefinite):
- Jobs: Paula is a teacher.
- Descriptions: Paris is an interesting city.
- First mention: I saw an old woman and a dog in the park.

**THE** (definite):
- Second mention: I saw a man and a dog. The man was old.
- Specific things: The children are in the garden.
- Only one of its kind: The moon looks beautiful today.
- Common places: We are going to the park.
- Superlatives: He is the best student in the class.
- Musical instruments: My daughter plays the violin.

**ZERO ARTICLE:**
- General concepts: I don't like children (= children in general).
- Meals: What time do you have breakfast?
- Years, months, days: 2005 was a great year.
- Media (TV, radio): There's a good film on TV.
- Languages & subjects: He doesn't speak English. I study biology.
- Some places (with go to): We went to the theater last week.

## Section 036 — Conjunctions: AND, BUT, OR, SO, BECAUSE

- **AND** (addition): He's a plumber, and she is a teacher.
- **BUT** (contrast): I like coffee, but I don't like tea.
- **OR** (alternative): Tea or coffee?
- **SO** (consequence): It was cold, so I turned on the heating.
- **BECAUSE** (cause): I turned on the heating because it was cold.

|---|---|---|

| Large quantity (neg./?) | many (books) | much (water) |

## Section 038 — WILL vs BE GOING TO

| | WILL | BE GOING TO |
|---|---|---|
| Positive | I'll pay. | He is going to pay. |
| Negative | I won't pay. | He isn't going to pay. |
| Question | Will you pay? | Is he going to pay? |

**WILL uses:**
- Predictions (we think it will happen): He will love your present. They won't win their next match.
- Instant decisions: "We don't have milk." "Don't worry, I'll buy some after work."

**BE GOING TO uses:**
- Predictions (we see it coming): Stop! We're going to crash! Look at those clouds, it's going to rain.
- Intentions and plans: I'm going to have a drink after work. We are going to get married soon.

## Section 039 — Present Perfect: Form

**Positive:** Subject + have/has + past participle. → I have seen her / I've seen her.
**Negative:** Subject + haven't/hasn't + past participle. → I haven't seen her.
**Question:** Have/Has + subject + past participle? → Have you seen her?
**Short answer:** Yes, I have. / No, she hasn't.

---

## Section 040 — Present Perfect: Meaning / Uses

**1. Past actions with present relevance** (we don't say when):
- She's had an accident. / I've passed the exam.

**2. Recent events** (with *just*, *already*, *yet*, *recently*):
- "Have you finished yet?" "Yes, I've already finished."
- Have you seen any good films recently?

**3. Negative — incomplete actions in the present period:**
- I haven't seen Ted today. Maybe he isn't feeling well.

**4. Life experiences** (we don't say when these events happened):
- We've been to Rome and Florence. / I haven't read that book.

**5. With EVER / NEVER and with superlatives + ever:**
- "Have you ever read it?" "I have never read it."
- This is the best food I've ever tried.

**6. Number of times so far:** I've seen this film three times.

**7. Unfinished situations** (started in the past, still continuing — often with *for*, *since*, *all my life*, *lately*):
- How long have you been here?
- We have been married for 20 years.
- I've lived in this house all my life.
- We have been very busy lately.
- I have been here all day.

## Section 041 — Defining Relative Clauses

**Relative pronouns:**
- **WHO** (people): That's the man **who** saved my life.
- **WHICH / THAT** (animals, things): He has a car **which/that** costs a fortune.

**Relative adverb:**
- **WHERE** (place): That's the place **where** I met my husband.

Defining relative clauses identify the noun — no commas are used.

## Section 044 — Comparative Adjectives

- **1 syllable** → add -er: tall → taller, old → older, fast → faster.
- **2 or more syllables** → more + adjective: interesting → more interesting, expensive → more expensive.

**Spelling notes:**
- Consonant + -e → add -r: large → larger.

- Consonant + -y → -ier: happy → happier.

**Irregular:** good → better, bad → worse, far → farther/further.

## Section 045 — Too, Too Much / Too Many, Enough

**TOO + adjective** = more than we want/need (negative).
- It's too cold. / These shoes are too small.

**TOO MUCH + uncountable noun:** There's too much sugar in my coffee.
**TOO MANY + plural countable noun:** There are too many people here.

**ENOUGH + noun** = the right quantity:
- We have enough chairs.

**Adjective + ENOUGH** = the right amount of that quality:
- He is tall enough to reach the shelf.

**NOT ENOUGH** = less than we need:
- I don't have enough money.

---

## Section 046 — Much, Many, Little, Few, Some, Any (consolidated)

**SOME / ANY:**
- SOME in positive sentences: I have some questions.
- ANY in negative and questions: Is there any sugar? / We didn't have any problems.
- SOME in offers / requests: Would you like some tea? / Can I have some tea?

**A LOT OF / LOTS OF** (countable and uncountable):
- With noun: He spends a lot of time watching TV.
- Without noun (at the end): They made cookies and I had lots / a lot. / I like her a lot.

**MANY / MUCH** (mainly in negatives / questions):
- There isn't much coffee. / Did they invite many people?
- TOO MANY / TOO MUCH in positives: There is too much salt in the soup.
- HOW MANY / HOW MUCH to ask about quantity: How many children do you have?

**FEW / LITTLE vs A FEW / A LITTLE:**
- **few / little** = almost none, not enough: There's little milk in the fridge — we need more.
- **a few / a little** = not a lot, but enough: There are a few in the fridge. / I speak a little English.

---

## Section 047 — Must vs Have To: Form

**MUST** is a modal auxiliary verb:
- Positive: We must call early.
- Negative: We mustn't arrive early. (NOT: We don't must…)
- Question: Must we call early? (not very common)
- Short answer: Yes, we must. / No, we mustn't.

**HAVE TO** is NOT an auxiliary — uses do/does/did:
- Positive: He has to wear a uniform.
- Negative: He doesn't have to wear a uniform.
- Question: Does he have to wear a uniform? (more common)
- Short answer: Yes, he does. / No, he doesn't.

**Tenses:** MUST has no past/future. Use HAD TO and WILL HAVE TO instead.
- ❌ Yesterday I must go to the dentist. → ✅ Yesterday I had to go to the dentist.
- ❌ I will must speak to her tomorrow. → ✅ I will have to speak to her tomorrow.

---

## Section 048 — Must vs Have To: Meaning

**MUST** = obligation that comes from the speaker (the speaker thinks it's necessary).
- Manager: You must be here at 7.
- Smoker: I must stop smoking. (he chose this himself)

**HAVE TO** = external obligation (someone/something else makes it necessary).
- Worker: I have to be there at 7. (the manager said so)
- Smoker: I have to stop smoking. (the doctor said so)

**MUSTN'T** = prohibition (you cannot do it):
- You mustn't wait here. (= it's not allowed)

**DON'T HAVE TO** = no obligation (you can, but you don't need to):
- You don't have to wait here. (= it's not necessary)

---

## Section 049 — First Conditional

Used to talk about something that **might happen in the future** if a condition exists.

**Structure:** If + present simple, … will + infinitive (or imperative / modal).
- If you study, you will pass.
- If he doesn't call, contact me immediately.
- If your room is tidy, you can go out.
- We will celebrate soon if we win.

**Rules:**
- No comma when the main clause comes first.
- Do NOT use *will* in the IF clause.
  - ❌ I'll help you if you will need me. → ✅ I'll help you if you need me.

---

## Section 050 — Future Time Clauses

After **when, as soon as, until, once, before, after, while** → use **present** (not future) to talk about the future.

- As soon as you arrive, call me.
- Until you all answer, nobody can leave.
- Before you come, you should text me.
- Once you live there, you'll need help.

**Rule:** No comma when the main clause comes first.
- ❌ When I will be 69, I will retire. → ✅ When I am 69, I will retire.

**Examples:**
- Subject: I play football.
- Object: He calls me every day.
- Possessive adjective (+ noun): This is my brother.
- Possessive pronoun (no noun): This pen is mine. / Those shoes are hers. / Is this book yours?

## Section 052 — Prepositions of Movement

- **TO** (destination): I took them to the beach. / I'd like to go to the cinema.
- **ALONG** (following a line/edge): We walked along the beach. / I ran along a narrow path.
- **FROM** (origin): She comes from Japan. / I ran from home to the gym.
- **THROUGH** (in one side and out the other): They drove through the tunnel.
- **INTO** (entering): I walked into the room. / Get into the car.
- **ACROSS** (one side to the other): Jessica swam across the river.
- **OUT OF** (leaving): They ran out of the room.
- **TOWARDS** (in the direction of): He walked towards me.
- **ONTO / ON** (onto a surface): I got on the first bus.
- **OVER** (above and across): He jumped over the fence.
- **OFF** (down from): The cat jumped off the table.
- **UNDER** (below): We drove under the bridge.
- **UP** (to a higher position): They went up the hill.
- **AROUND** (in a circular path): The moon goes around the earth.
- **DOWN** (to a lower position): I rode my bike down the mountain.
- **PAST** (by and beyond): He walked past us without seeing us.

## Section 053 — Future Forms (overview)

**WILL:**
- Predictions (we think): He will be a good doctor.
- Instant decisions: A: "We don't have sugar." B: "I'll buy some then."
- Promises and refusals: I will help you but I won't lend you any money.
- Offers / suggestions: I'll carry your bag. / Shall we eat out today?
- Requests: Will you open the door, please?

**BE GOING TO:**
- Predictions (we see evidence): Slow down! We're going to have an accident! / Look at the clouds — it's going to rain.
- Plans / intentions: Are you going to watch the game tonight?

**PRESENT SIMPLE** — timetabled events:
- The train leaves at 4 o'clock. / The exams start next week.
- After future time clauses: I'll retire when I am 70.

**PRESENT CONTINUOUS** — fixed arrangements:
- I'm flying to New York tomorrow morning. / I'm seeing the dentist at 6. / We are getting married next week.

**MAY / MIGHT** — future possibility:
- I might go to the party tomorrow; I'm not sure.
- Temperatures might go up next week.

---

## Section 054 — Present Perfect Continuous: Form

**Positive:** Subject + have/has + been + verb-ing. → I have been working / She has been working.
**Negative:** haven't / hasn't been + verb-ing.
**Question:** Have/Has + subject + been + verb-ing? → Have you been working?
**Short answer:** Yes, I have. / No, she hasn't.

---

## Section 055 — Present Perfect Continuous: Meaning

**1. Duration from past until now** (situations that haven't finished or have just finished, with action verbs):
- Have you been crying?
- I have been painting the bedroom.

**2. Often with present results:**
- She has been calling you for days. (her actions explain something now)

**3. Repeated actions from past until now.**

**4. Often used with time expressions:** for, since, how long, all…, lately.
- We have been studying since you left this morning.
- I've been trying to talk to you all evening.
- She hasn't been sleeping very well lately.

---

## Section 056 — During vs For vs While

**DURING + noun** (at the same time as a noun event):
- Some people got lost during the journey.
- I will finish the novel during the summer break.
- Most burglaries happen during the night.

**FOR + time period** (length of time):
- We have known each other for a long time.
- I waited for more than an hour, but I got tired and went home.
- He worked in this company for over 30 years.

**WHILE + subject + verb** (at the same time as another action):
- I had tea while you were in the shower.
- I always listen to the radio while I'm cleaning.
- You shouldn't use your phone while you are driving.

---

## Section 057 — Narrative Tenses: Past Simple, Continuous & Perfect

**Past simple** — completed actions:
- We went to the zoo last week.
- Sorry, what did you say?
- Past habits/states: We often went to the pub after work.
- Duration finished in the past: I played football for 20 years.
- Narrative: events in chronological order. She opened the door, looked at us, and went to her room.

**Past continuous** — actions in progress at a moment in the past:
- Yesterday at 10 p.m. I was sleeping.
- When Natasha opened the door, we were talking about her.
- Narrative: setting the scene. It was getting dark, and I was walking to the pub when…

**Past perfect** — earlier in the past:
- When I met her, I had never been in a serious relationship.
- He noticed that I had cleaned the car. It was clean and shiny.
- Duration up to a past point: When she died, they had been married for 48 years.

---

## Section 058 — Narrative Tenses: Example Paragraph

> Last night, I **was walking** home when I **saw** a car running very fast towards a red light. I **looked** at the driver and I could see that he **was talking** on his phone. He **didn't stop** at the traffic light, and another car **crashed** into him. When the police **arrived**, they **asked** me what **had happened** and I **told** them that the driver **hadn't seen** the traffic light because he **was using** his phone.

- **Past simple** → main events in chronological order.
- **Past continuous** → set the scene / describe actions in progress.
- **Past perfect** → events that happened earlier in the past.

---

## Section 059 — Used To, Be / Get Used To

**USED TO + infinitive** — past habits or past states that are no longer true:
- When I was a teenager, I used to go dancing every weekend.
- He used to be blond as a child.
- I used to love chocolate.

**BE USED TO + -ing / noun** — be accustomed to (it's no longer strange or difficult):
- I'm still not used to driving on the left.
- At first it was hard to get up at 6 a.m., but now I'm used to it.

**GET USED TO + -ing / noun** — the process of becoming accustomed:
- I'm still getting used to working night shifts.
- It might be difficult at first, but you will get used to driving on the left.

**USED TO vs USUALLY:** USED TO is past only. For present habits, use USUALLY.
- ❌ We use to eat out on Saturday. → ✅ We usually eat out on Saturday.

**Form of USED TO:**
- Positive: He used to play football.
- Negative: He didn't use to play football. (no -d)
- Question: Did he use to play football? (no -d)

---

## Section 060 — Passive Voice: All Tenses

The passive uses **BE + past participle**. Use it when the doer is unknown, unimportant, or you want to focus on the action/result.

| Tense | Active | Passive |
|---|---|---|
| Present simple | They take the photos. | The photos are taken. |
| Present continuous | They are taking the photos. | The photos are being taken. |
| Past simple | They took the photos. | The photos were taken. |
| Past continuous | They were taking the photos. | The photos were being taken. |
| Present perfect | They have taken the photos. | The photos have been taken. |
| Past perfect | They had taken the photos. | The photos had been taken. |
| Future (will) | They will take the photos. | The photos will be taken. |
| Be going to | They are going to take the photos. | The photos are going to be taken. |
| Infinitive | to take | to be taken |
| Perfect infinitive | to have taken | to have been taken |

You can use **GET** instead of BE in informal English: The photos get taken / got broken.

## Section 061 — Modal Verbs of Deduction (Present)

**MUST** — we are sure something is true (≈ 95%):
- She must be in the garden. (= I'm sure she is.)
- They must know each other.

**CAN'T** — we are sure something is NOT true:
- She can't be his mother — she's too young.
- He's just left. He can't be too far.

**MAY / MIGHT (NOT)** — 30–50% sure (maybe true, maybe not):
- He may/might be dead.
- He may/might not remember who you are.

**Important:**
- ❌ He's just left. He mustn't be too far. → ✅ He can't be too far.
- ❌ He isn't here. He can be at home. → ✅ He might be at home.

**With continuous (action in progress):**
- They are in Ibiza. They must be having a great time.
- You're always hungry. You might not be eating enough protein.
- Air pollution may be making people unhappy.

---

## Section 062 — Third Conditional

Used to talk about **past hypothetical or unreal situations**.

**Structure:** If + past perfect, … would/could/might have + past participle.
- If you had come to class, you would have passed the exam. (you didn't come, you didn't pass)
- If he hadn't had his helmet on, he might have died. (he wore it, so he didn't die)
- He might have died if he hadn't had his helmet on.

**Rule:** No comma when the main clause comes first.

---

## Section 063 — All, Both: Quantifiers

**ALL + noun** (in general): All plants need water.
**ALL (OF) THE/MY + noun** (specific): All (of) the plants in the garden died.
**BOTH (OF THE/MY) + noun** (two specific things): Both (of the) students passed the exam.
**ALL / BOTH OF + us/you/them:** All / both of them were at the party.

**Mid-position:**
- After be (main verb): We were all/both happy.
- After auxiliary: They have all/both been arrested.
- After subject in questions: Have they all/both finished?
- Before main verb: They all/both went to the event.
- After pronoun-object: He asked us all/both to stay. / You ate them all/both!

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ All/Both of students → ✅ All/Both of the students
- ❌ The both students → ✅ Both (of the) students
- ❌ All/Both of we/they → ✅ All/Both of us/them

---

## Section 064 — Both, Either, Neither

**BOTH** (positive — one AND the other):
- Jane and Margaret passed the exam.
- Both Jane and Margaret passed.
- Both of them passed.

**EITHER** (one OR the other, positive or negative verb):
- Either Sue or her sister has been here.
- You can fish from either side of the river.
- Either is a good purchase.
- Choose one of the cars — either is OK.

**NEITHER** (NOT one and NOT the other — with positive verb):
- Neither Sue nor her sister has been here.
- Neither of the candidates is a good option. (or *are*)
- Neither candidate is a good option.
- I like neither of them.

---

## Section 065 — Any, No, None

**ANY** (in negatives & questions):
- I don't have any friends. / Is there any milk?
- anybody / anyone, anything, anywhere

**ANY in positives** (= it doesn't matter which):
- You can come any weekend. / Any idiot would know how to use this phone.

**NO + noun** (positive verb, negative meaning):
- I have no friends. / We use no fertilizer.
- nobody / no-one, nothing, nowhere

**NONE** (without a noun):
- "How many friends do you have?" "None."
- I thought I had some money but I have none.

**NONE OF + plural noun / object pronoun** (verb can be singular or plural):
- None of the students is / are from France.

---

## Section 066 — So, Such, So Much, So Many

**SO + adjective / adverb (+ that):**
- You are so stubborn.
- Why do you drive so slowly?
- He drives so slowly that it's dangerous.

**SO MUCH + uncountable noun (+ that):**
- I didn't know she had so much money.
- We cook so much food that we have to throw half away.

**SO MANY + plural noun (+ that):**
- We couldn't walk among so many fans.
- There were so many fans that we didn't really enjoy it.

**SUCH + (adjective) + uncountable / plural (+ that):**
- We had such terrible weather!
- She said such nice things that we were all in tears.

**SUCH A + (adjective) + singular countable noun (+ that):**
- It was such a party! It was real fun.
- He is such a liar that no-one trusts him.

**Common mistake:** Don't use SO + adjective + noun.
- ❌ He is so nice person! → ✅ He is such a nice person! / He is so nice.

## Section 068 — Position of Adverbs

**Initial position** (before the subject):
- Comment adverbs: Luckily, nobody got hurt. / Unfortunately, we arrived late.
- Frequency (less common here): Sometimes he can be stubborn. / Usually we eat out at the weekend.
- Time: Just before the wedding, they split up.
- Manner (literary only): Carefully, she opened the box.

**Mid position** (before main verb / after BE / after first auxiliary):
- Manner (literary or with passive): She carefully opened the box. / They were seriously injured.
- Frequency: I usually work on Saturdays. / She's hardly ever late.
- Most other adverbs: We had just met when he died. / I've already seen this film.

**Final position** (end of the clause):
- Manner: She always speaks very quickly. / She can dance salsa marvellously.
- Place: They met by chance in England. / I haven't seen him anywhere.
- Time: It rained quite heavily yesterday. / They'll be here soon.

**Order at the end:** manner + place + time. → They met by chance in England in 2012.

## Section 069 — Future Continuous

**Form:** Subject + will be + verb-ing.

**Use 1 — actions in progress at a certain time in the future:**
- This time next week, I will be travelling to Paris.
- Tomorrow at 10, you'll be doing your exam.
- When you get off the train, I'll be waiting at the platform.

**Use 2 — future plans / arrangements** (≈ present continuous or be going to):
- We'll be coming next weekend.
- I will be leaving tomorrow at 8 a.m.

**Time expressions:** this time tomorrow / next week, at 9 tomorrow, in two weeks (' time), when…

## Section 070 — Future Perfect

**Form:** Subject + will have + past participle.

**Use 1 — actions finished before some time in the future:**
- I will have finished my work by this time tomorrow.

**Use 2 — duration of a situation up to a future point** (future perfect continuous: will have been + -ing):
- By 2030 I will have been married for 20 years.

**Time expressions:** by tomorrow / next week / 2050, by this time tomorrow, by the time + subject + verb, in two weeks' time, when, before.

## Section 071 — Zero Conditional

- If the food is out of date, I don't eat it.

- If you are talking, I can't concentrate.

We can replace IF with WHEN without changing the meaning:
- When you are talking, I can't concentrate.

No comma when the main clause comes first.

## Section 075 — I Wish / If Only

**+ past simple** — things we'd like to be different now/in the future:
- I wish I was/were a bit taller. (informal: was / formal: were)
- I wish I could be there for you tomorrow.

**+ past perfect** — things that happened in the past and we regret:
- I wish I hadn't quit my job two years ago.
- If only we hadn't wasted all that money.

**+ would** — things that annoy us; we'd like them to change/stop:
- I wish you would stop biting your nails. I hate it!
- If only it would stop raining!

**Note:** Do NOT use *I wish I would*… to wish about yourself.

## Section 076 — Past Modals of Deduction

**MUST HAVE + past participle** — we're sure it WAS true:
- She must have been in the garden when I called.

**CAN'T HAVE / COULDN'T HAVE + past participle** — we're sure it was NOT true:
- You can't have seen her. She was at home.
- You couldn't have seen her.

**MAY / MIGHT / COULD HAVE + past participle** — maybe (not) true (30–50%):
- He may have taken a different train. He'll call.
- He might not have heard you.
- He could be hurt.

**SHOULD HAVE / OUGHT TO HAVE + past participle** — expected outcome / advice:
- You should have packed / ought to have packed your things. You're going to be late.
- You shouldn't have done / ought not to have done that. It was rude.

(Present-meaning equivalents: must be, can't be, may/might/could be, should be / ought to be.)

## Section 077 — Would Rather & Prefer

**WOULD RATHER / WOULD SOONER + infinitive (no *to*):**
- I'd rather have tea than coffee.

**WOULD RATHER + subject + past simple** (to talk about the present/future):
- I'd rather you took a taxi tonight.

**WOULD RATHER + subject + past perfect** (to talk about the past):
- I'd rather you had taken a taxi last night.

**(WOULD) PREFER + to-infinitive / object + to-infinitive:**
- I'd prefer to stay in a hotel near the airport.
- I'd prefer you to stay in a hotel near the airport.

**Showing the choices:**
- I'd rather be too early than be too late.
- I'd prefer to go with dad rather than / instead of staying here.

**General preference (no specific choice):** PREFER + -ing.
- I prefer running to cycling.

## Section 080 — Distancing: The Passive with Reporting Verbs

Used to distance yourself from information ("someone else said this, not me"). Common reporting verbs: agree, announce, believe, claim, consider, expect, hope, know, report, say, suggest, think, understand.

**Structure 1 — IT + passive reporting verb + (that) clause:**

## Section 081 — Have / Get Something Done (Causative)

**HAVE + object + past participle** — someone else does the action for you.
- I'm having my computer repaired.
- We've had our kitchen redecorated.
- We had our car stolen. (something bad happened to us)
- They had their house destroyed by the fire.

**GET + object + past participle** — informal equivalent.
- I should get my eyes tested.
- I've got my car repaired.

**HAVE + person + infinitive** (mention the person who does it):
- We are having the architect redesign the ground floor.

**GET + person + to-infinitive** (similar but informal):
- I got a mechanic to take a look at the car.

**Form notes:** HAVE here is NOT an auxiliary verb — use do/did in negatives and questions.
- ❌ We hadn't our house painted. → ✅ We didn't have our house painted.
- ❌ Had you your house painted? → ✅ Did you have your house painted?

## Section 082 — Reporting Verbs: Patterns Overview

Common reporting verbs are followed by different structures:

**+ to-infinitive:** agree, claim, offer, promise, refuse, swear, threaten.

**+ person + to-infinitive:** advise, ask, beg, convince, encourage, forbid, invite, order, persuade, recommend, remind, tell, warn.

**+ (preposition) + -ing:** accuse sb of, admit, apologise to sb for, blame sb for, complain about, confess to, deny, insist on, recommend, regret, suggest.

**+ that + clause:** admit, agree, answer, claim, complain, confess, deny, explain, insist, promise, regret, say, suggest.

Some verbs (marked with *) take more than one pattern: He admitted stealing the money. / He admitted that he had stolen the money.

## Section 084 — Whatever, Whichever, Whenever, Wherever, Whoever, However

Each means "any …" or "it doesn't matter …":

- **WHATEVER** — anything / it doesn't matter what.
  - We can do whatever we want now.
- **WHICHEVER** — any one of / it doesn't matter which.
  - Use one of those two pillows, whichever you prefer.
- **WHENEVER** — at any time / it doesn't matter when.
  - You can come whenever you want.
- **WHEREVER** — to/at/in any place / it doesn't matter where.
  - Leave it wherever you like.
- **WHOEVER** — any person / it doesn't matter who.
  - We have to find whoever did this.
- **HOWEVER** — in any way / it doesn't matter how.
  - You can use the money however you like.

## Section 085 — All, Most: Quantifiers

**ALL + noun** (in general): All plants need water.
**ALL (OF) THE + noun** (specific): All (of) the plants in the garden died.
**ALL OF + us/you/them:** All of them were at the party.

**ALL in mid position:**
- Before main verb: We all went to the event.
- After BE / auxiliary: They were all happy.

**MOST OF THE/MY + noun** (specific): Most of the students in my class are French.
**MOST OF + pronoun:** Most of them are French.

## Section 091 — Future in the Past

## Section 092 — Future in the Past: Other Expressions

| Present form | Past form | Meaning |
|---|---|---|

## Section 093 — Other Ways to Express the Future

## Section 094 — Verbs with Two Objects

Verbs like *give, send, show, tell* can take two objects: an indirect (person) and a direct (thing).

**Two orders:**
- Subject + verb + indirect + direct: Paul sent my friends a picture.
- Subject + verb + direct + to/for + indirect: Paul sent a picture to my friends.

**Verbs with TO:** give, lend, offer, pass, promise, read, sell, send, show, tell.
**Verbs with FOR:** bring, buy, cook, find, get, make.

## Section 095 — Passive with Two-Object Verbs

When a verb has two objects, the passive can be built on either object — the indirect (person) is preferred.

Active: Paul sent **my friends** a picture. / Paul sent a picture **to my friends**.

Passive (person as subject): My friends were sent a picture.
Passive (thing as subject): A picture was sent to my friends.

Same verb groups: TO (give, lend, offer, …) and FOR (bring, buy, cook, …).

## Section 100 — Present Simple vs Continuous: Meaning

**Present Simple:**
- Habits / regular actions: I wash my hair every day. He usually gets up very early.
- Facts, permanent situations, states: I have one brother. He lives in Paris. / Water boils at 100 degrees.
- With **stative verbs**: Give me the money. I need it now. / You look sad.
- Signal words: always, never, often, sometimes, every day, once a month, twice a week.

**Present Continuous:**
- Actions in progress now: I can't talk — I'm brushing my teeth.
- Actions in progress around now: I'm reading a new book. I love it!
- Signal words: now, at the moment, these days, this week, this month.

**Stative verbs** (not normally used in continuous): hear, see, smell, look, seem, sound, believe, consider, like, love, hate, prefer, think, have, own, belong, be, need, mean, remember, want.

## Section 102 — Second Conditional

Used to talk about **present or future hypothetical / unreal situations**.

**Structure:** If + past simple, … would/could/might + infinitive.
- If I won the lottery, I would buy a yacht.
- If I had a better salary, I could travel more.
- If I were you, I might wait before making a decision.
- If it weren't for his help, I might be dead now.
- I would buy a yacht if I won the lottery.

**Rules:**
- No comma when the main clause comes first.
- You can use *I/he/she/it was* (informal) or *were* (more formal).
- BUT always use *were* in **If I were you** (giving advice).

## Section 103 — First vs Second Conditional

**FIRST CONDITIONAL** — present/future possible situations.
- If + present, … will / imperative / modal.
- If you study, you will pass the exam.
- If he doesn't call, you should tell me immediately.

**SECOND CONDITIONAL** — present/future unreal situations.
- If + past, … would/could/might + infinitive.
- If I won the lottery, I would buy a yacht.
- If I had a better salary, I could travel more.
- If I were you, I might wait before making a decision.

---

## Section 104 — Second Conditional (extended)

**Structure:** If + past simple OR past continuous, … would/could/might + infinitive / be + -ing.
- If I won the lottery, I would buy a yacht.
- If you weren't working here, you'd be in prison.
- If it weren't for his help, I might not be talking to you now.
- I might not be talking to you now if it weren't for his help.

Same rules: no comma if main clause first; use *were* in "If I were you".

## Section 117 — Already, Still, Yet

**ALREADY** = something happens earlier than expected. Used in positives and questions.
- I am already home. (You expected me later.)
- I've already eaten.
- Have you already finished? That was fast!

**STILL** = a situation is continuing; it hasn't changed or stopped. Often shows surprise/upset, especially in negatives.
- Is it still raining? / She is still living with her parents.
- I'm still waiting!
- I still haven't found my wallet.

**YET** — used in negatives ("not happened") and questions ("has it happened?"):
- She hasn't called yet. / I haven't finished my homework yet. / He is not here yet.
- Is she here yet? / Have you finished your homework yet?

**Compare:**
- Have you already finished? = I didn't expect you to finish so soon.
- Have you finished yet? = I don't know — I want to know.
- She still hasn't called. = I'm surprised/upset.
- She hasn't called yet. = It's OK, I guess she'll call soon.

## Section 118 — Prepositions of Time: AT / IN / ON

**AT:**
- Times of day: at 4 o'clock, at 10:30, at noon, at midnight.
- Mealtimes: at lunchtime, at dinnertime.
- Holidays / weekend: at Christmas, at Easter, at the weekend.
- Expressions: at present, at the moment, at night.

**IN:**
- Months: in April.
- Seasons: in the summer, in the spring.
- Years: in 1990, in the 80s.
- Centuries: in the 20th century.
- Long periods: in the ice age, in the present, in the past.
- Parts of the day: in the morning, in the afternoon, in the evening (BUT *at night*).

**ON:**
- Days: on Tuesday, on Saturday, on my birthday, on Christmas Day, on Halloween.
- Dates: on 15th June, on 20th May, on our anniversary.

## Section 119 — Clauses of Contrast, Purpose & Reason

**Contrast — ALTHOUGH / EVEN THOUGH + subject + verb:**

**Contrast — HOWEVER + comma (separates two sentences):**
- We played well. However, we lost.
- I eat better. However, I keep gaining weight.

**Contrast — IN SPITE OF / DESPITE + noun / -ing / the fact that:**
- We went out in spite of / despite the rain.
- We went out in spite of / despite the fact that it was raining.

**Purpose — TO / IN ORDER TO / SO AS TO + infinitive:**
- The new student worked hard to pass the test.
- We were quiet so as not to wake up the baby.

**Purpose — SO THAT + clause:**
- We left early so that we could park the car.

**Purpose — FOR + noun:**
- They went to the pub for a drink.

**Reason — AS / SINCE / BECAUSE + subject + verb:**
- As/Since it was raining so heavily, we stayed.
- We didn't go because it was raining heavily.

**Reason — BECAUSE OF / DUE TO + noun:**
- We didn't go because of the heavy rain.
- We didn't go due to the heavy rain. (more formal)

**MAKE** is used for:
- **Food and drink preparation:** make lunch / dinner, make a cake, make coffee, make a sandwich.
- **Speaking, sounds, relationships:** make a promise, make a phone call, make a joke, make a suggestion, make an excuse, make a noise, make friends, make sure, make an apology, make a complaint, make fun of sb, make contacts.
- **Arrangements and decisions:** make an appointment, make plans, make a reservation, make a list, make a choice, make a decision, make a mistake, make a mess.
- **Money:** make money, make $10 an hour, make a living, make a profit, make an offer.

---

## Section 127 — Stative (Non-action) Verbs

Stative verbs describe states, not actions. They are NOT normally used in continuous tenses.

**Possession:** belong, own, have (= possess), possess, be, weigh.
**Opinion:** know, agree, believe, think (= believe), imagine, understand.
**Likes / dislikes / emotions:** like, dislike, love, prefer, want, need, wish.
**Perception and senses:** sound, hear, seem, see, appear, look, taste, smell, feel, measure.

**Note:** Some perception verbs (see, taste, smell, look, feel, appear) can be **dynamic** when they describe a voluntary action.
- This soup tastes horrible. (= perception → simple)
- I am tasting the soup. (= voluntary action — checking the flavour → continuous)

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ I am wanting to eat. → ✅ I want to eat.
- ❌ He was looking happy. → ✅ He looked happy.

---

## Section 128 — Don't Need To vs Needn't / Didn't Need To vs Needn't Have

**DON'T NEED TO / NEEDN'T + infinitive** — absence of necessity (general):
- People don't need to have a degree to get a good job.
- You don't need to clean the windows. / You needn't clean the windows.

**DIDN'T NEED TO + infinitive** — something wasn't necessary, and may or may not have happened.
- He got better and didn't need to go to the doctor. (so he didn't go)
- Thanks, but you didn't need to bring wine. (could mean either)

**NEEDN'T HAVE + past participle** — something happened, but it wasn't necessary.
- Thanks, but you needn't have brought wine. (you brought it, and it wasn't necessary)

---

## Section 129 — Phrasal Verbs: Separable / Inseparable

A phrasal verb = verb + particle (e.g., wake up).

**Intransitive (no object — inseparable):** verb + particle, never separated.
- What time do you get up? / I'd like to go out. / Energy prices will go up.

**Transitive separable** — the object can go before OR after the particle, but pronouns MUST go between:
- Put on your jacket. / Put your jacket on. / Put it on. (NOT: Put on it.)
- I took off my shoes. / I took my shoes off. / I took them off. (NOT: I took off them.)

**Transitive inseparable** — verb + particle + object (or pronoun); they cannot be separated:
- I am looking for my keys. / I am looking for them. (NOT: I am looking them for.)

---

## Section 130 — No Longer, Any Longer, Anymore

All mean "something has stopped happening or being true". She **doesn't work here** anymore = She no longer works here.

**NO LONGER** with positive verbs, in mid position (before main verb / after BE / after modal-auxiliary):
- I no longer eat meat.
- We are no longer in school.
- We can no longer be friends.

**ANY LONGER / ANYMORE** with negative verbs, in end position:
- I don't eat meat any longer / anymore.

**ANYMORE (time) vs ANY MORE (quantity):**
- ✅ I don't eat chicken anymore. (time)
- ✅ Do you want any more chicken? (quantity)

---

## Section 131 — On Time vs In Time / At the End vs In the End

**ON TIME** = punctually, according to schedule:
- I hope the flight arrives on time.
- The meeting started on time.

**IN TIME** = soon enough, not too late for something:
- The card arrived in time for his birthday.
- We arrived at the theater in time to get good seats.

**AT THE END** = at the final moment / location of something:
- There is a gift shop at the end of the road.
- The school year finishes at the end of June.

**IN THE END** = finally, eventually (after some process):
- In the end, we decided to go for the cheaper option.
- We did our best, and in the end, we succeeded.

---

## Section 132 — Inversion After Negative Adverbials (formal)

When a negative adverbial is placed at the beginning of a sentence for emphasis, **the auxiliary moves before the subject** (like in a question).

**HARDLY / BARELY / SCARCELY … WHEN … / NO SOONER … THAN …:**
- Hardly had we arrived home when we heard the news.
- Barely had I opened the book when Mark put on the music.
- No sooner had the shop opened its doors than it went bankrupt.

**ONLY (in various combinations):**
- Only if we invest more money can we save the company.
- Only when I sleep can I forget about the incident.
- Only now can I understand what really happened.

**NOT ONLY … BUT ALSO / NOT ONCE / NOT SINCE / NOT UNTIL:**
- Not only will you get a good result, but you will also be the best.
- Not once did she look at me at the party.
- Not since I was a child have I had such a great time.
- Not until I see her again will I be happy.

**UNDER NO CIRCUMSTANCES / IN NO WAY / NOWHERE / NO WAY (informal):**
- Under no circumstances should we allow this to happen.
- In no way am I related to this man.
- Nowhere were the people more excited than in that stadium.

**NEVER / RARELY / SELDOM / LITTLE:**
- Never (before) have I seen such a stupid man.
- Rarely did they speak to each other.
- Seldom have we seen him in his office.
- Little did we know about him when we hired him.

## Section 133 — Do / To Do / Doing — Infinitive vs Gerund

**TO + infinitive:**
- Express purpose: I'll call her to tell her what happened.
- After too / enough + adjective: He isn't old enough to vote.
- After would + like/love/hate/prefer: I'd like to go to the cinema.
- After most verbs + person: He told me to wait.
- After what/where/when/why + to-inf: I don't know what to do.
- After certain verbs (see list below).

**Infinitive without TO:**
- After modal verbs (will, can, must, should): I can't lend you money.
- After make / let + person: He made us wait.

**-ing form:**
- As the subject: Cheating is not allowed.
- After a preposition: I'm tired of waiting.
- After spend/waste + time/money: He spends a lot of time playing.
- After like/love/hate/prefer/don't mind: I love cycling.
- After certain verbs (see list below).

**Verbs + to-infinitive:** afford, agree, appear, be able, choose, decide, deserve, expect, happen, help, hesitate, hope, learn, make, manage, offer, plan, pretend, promise, refuse, seem, teach, tend, want.

**Verbs + -ing:** admit, avoid, deny, enjoy, fancy, feel like, finish, keep, imagine, involve, mind, miss, practise, recommend, regret, spend, suggest.

## Section 134 — Gerunds and Infinitives: Complex Forms

|  | Infinitive (Active) | Infinitive (Passive) | -ing (Active) | -ing (Passive) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Simple — present/future | (to) do — I need to talk to John. | (to) be done — He wants to be sent home. | doing — She regretted marrying so young. | being done — He denied being offered a bribe. |
| Continuous — in progress now | (to) be doing — They seem to be having fun. | — | — | — |

| Perfect continuous — duration from earlier | (to) have been doing — They're thought to have been hiding here. | — | — | — |

## Section 135 — Infinitive or Gerund — Verb Patterns (extended)

**TO + infinitive** is used for:
- Purpose: I'll call her to tell her what happened.
- After certain adjectives: I'm glad to know you are OK. / I'm so sorry to hear that.
- After it + be + adjective: It's fantastic to be here. / It was so nice of her to say that.
- After nouns derived from to-inf verbs: the decision to increase taxes, his father's advice to stay calm.
- After would + like/love/hate/prefer: I'd love to see the views.
- In set expressions: to be honest, to tell you the truth, to begin with.
- After enough / too / a lot / etc.: You are too young to be here. / There's a lot to do.
- After something/anything/someone/anywhere: There's nothing to do.
- After what/where/when/why + to-inf: I don't know what to do.
- After superlatives & first/second: He is the best player to ever play in Scotland. / I was the first to arrive.
- After certain verbs (afford, agree, decide, etc.).

**Infinitive without TO:**
- After modal verbs: He should be home by now.
- After make / let / help + object: He made us wait. / They won't let me express myself.
- After had better / would rather / would sooner: You'd better not say anything.
- After hear / listen / notice / see + object — for a short or complete action: I saw them kiss in the park. / I heard someone shout your name.

**-ing form:**
- As the subject: Cheating is not allowed.
- After a preposition: I'm tired of waiting.
- After spend / waste + time/money: He spends a lot of time playing with his kids.
- In expressions: it's no use / no good, it's (not) worth, there's no point (in), can't help, have difficulty (in), have trouble.
- After hear / listen / notice / see / watch — for a long or in-progress action: I saw them kissing. / I heard them arguing.
- After certain verbs (admit, avoid, deny, enjoy, etc.).

## Section 136 — When I Do vs When I Have Done (Future Time Clauses)

**Present simple after WHEN** = during OR after the event:
- I'll move out when I find a job. (= after)
- We'll talk when we have dinner. (= during)
- When I call Tom, I'll invite him. (= during the call)

**Present perfect after WHEN** = clearly AFTER the event:
- I'll move out when I've found a job. (= after I find one)
- We'll talk when we've had dinner. (= after dinner)
- You can't play until you've done all your homework. (= after you finish)

You CANNOT use present perfect if the second action happens DURING the first (e.g., "When I call Tom, I'll invite him" — the invitation happens during the call).

## Section 137 — Compound Adjectives

Different types of compound adjective patterns:

- **Adjective + present participle:** long-lasting, easy-going, good-looking, slow-moving.
- **Adjective + past participle:** old-fashioned, short-lived, ready-made, deep-fried.
- **Adverb / preposition + present participle:** long-standing, last-minute, English-speaking, heart-breaking.
- **Adverb / preposition + past participle:** short-handed, high-powered, sun-dried, ice-cold, world-famous, fat-free, oil-rich.
- **Adverb + past participle (-ly):** well-educated, brightly-lit, highly-respected.
- **Number + noun:** five-star, one-way, six-pack, two-bedroom.
- **Number + body part + -ed:** eight-legged, one-eyed, one-armed, two-headed.

(See Section 113 for number + singular noun compounds.)

## Section 138 — Would Rather & Would Sooner (extended)

**Same subject — WOULD RATHER / WOULD SOONER + infinitive (no *to*):**
- I would rather/sooner sit inside.
- He'd rather stay home. / I'd sooner have tea.
- Negative: I'd rather not have chicken again.
- Question: Would you rather go by taxi?
- Short answer: "Would you like to watch a film?" "Thanks, but I'd rather not."

**Different subject — WOULD RATHER / SOONER + subject + past simple** (present/future meaning):
- I'd rather you sat with me today.
- I'd rather we left tomorrow.
- Negative: I'd rather you didn't come over.
- Question: Would you rather he came here? / Would you rather we waited?
- Short answer: "Thanks, but I'd rather you didn't."

**Common mistake:** Use *I'd rather wait* (same subject) or *I'd rather + subject + past* (different subject).
- ❌ I'd rather you wait here. → ✅ I'd rather you waited here.

## Section 139 — DO vs MAKE (extended)

Same content as Section 126 with additional collocations:
- **DO:** add do an errand, do chores, do sb a favour. BUT: *make your bed*.
- **MAKE:** add make a reservation, make a mess, make a fuss, make an apology, make a complaint, make contacts, make fun of sb, make sure.

## Section 140 — Would Like vs Like

**WOULD LIKE** = "want" (specific moment / polite request).
- Would you like some cake? (= Do you want some cake?)
- Would you like to go to the beach? (= Do you want to go?)
- I'd like some tea. / I'd like to watch TV.

**LIKE** = enjoy / think something is good (general preference).
- Do you like cake? (= Do you think cake is good?)
- Do you like going to the beach? / Do you like to go to the beach? (= Do you enjoy it?)
- I like tea. / I like watching TV. / I like to watch TV.

**Note:** *Like* + -ing or to-infinitive both work for general enjoyment. *Would like* takes only to-infinitive.

---

## Section 141 — There vs It

**THERE** — used to say something exists / is happening, with a noun:
- There are some cups on the table.
- There is a party tomorrow.
- There is a problem.
- There was an accident.

**IT** — used to talk about times, dates, weather, distance, and to refer to something already mentioned:
- It's 4th of July. / It's my birthday. / It's four o'clock. / It's very late.
- It rained a lot yesterday. / It's four degrees.
- How far is it? / It's 2 kilometres from the hotel.
- It's nice to see you again. / It was difficult to find the hotel.
- There's a party tonight. It will be fun! / We saw a snake. It was very big.

**Common mistakes:** Every sentence needs one subject + verb.
- ❌ The worst part it's the speaking. → ✅ The worst part is the speaking.
- ❌ I love their music. Is fantastic! → ✅ I love their music. It's fantastic!

## Section 142 — Subject Questions

When the question word IS the subject, **do NOT use an auxiliary** — just use the verb directly.

**Object question** (subject is someone else): Who did Peter call? — Peter called me.
**Subject question** (the question word is the subject): Who called you? — Peter called me.

**Structure:** Question word (= subject) + main verb + object?
- Who called you? (NOT: Who did call you?)
- What happened? / Who lives here? / Which team won?

---

## Section 143 — Purpose: TO & FOR

**Saying WHY we do something:**
- **TO + infinitive:** I went to the shop to buy some milk.
- **FOR + noun:** I went to the shop for some milk.

**Saying WHAT something is used for:**
- **TO + infinitive:** These gloves are used to protect your hands.
- **FOR + noun:** These gloves are used for protection.
- **FOR + -ing verb:** These gloves are used for protecting your hands.

---

## Section 144 — Before, After, While, As Soon As, When, Once

**BEFORE:**
- + noun: I brush my teeth before breakfast.
- + -ing: I brush my teeth before having breakfast.
- + subject + verb: I brush my teeth before I have breakfast.

**AFTER:**
- + noun / -ing / subject + verb (same patterns as BEFORE).
- For consecutive actions, use *then, after that*:
  - I get up and have a shower. After / Then / After that, I have breakfast.

**WHILE + subject + verb** (during another action):
- I read the newspaper while I was waiting.

**WHEN / AS SOON AS / ONCE + subject + verb** (similar meaning):
- As soon as / When / Once I get home, I'll finish my homework.

---

## Section 145 — However, Although, Because, So

**Contrast — ALTHOUGH + subject + verb** (mid-sentence or at the beginning):
- We had fun, although we didn't like the hotel.
- Although they played well, they lost.

**Contrast — HOWEVER + comma** (between two sentences):
- We had fun; however, we didn't like the hotel.
- They played well. However, they lost.

**Reason — BECAUSE + subject + verb:**
- They cancelled the concert because it was raining.
- I'm fit because I work out.

**Reason — BECAUSE OF + noun:**
- They cancelled the concert because of the rain.
- They had to close because of the crisis.

**Result — SO + subject + verb:**
- It was very late, so we took a taxi home.
- "She doesn't want to do it." "So do it yourself!"

---

## Section 146 — Present Continuous vs Be Going To (Future)

Both can express future plans. The difference:

**Present continuous** — we've made arrangements; it's a definite plan with time/place decided:
- I'm leaving very early tomorrow. (I've already arranged the trip.)

**BE GOING TO** — emphasises the speaker's **intention**; arrangements may or may not exist yet:
- I'm going to leave very early tomorrow. (My intention — maybe no specific arrangements yet.)

If the plan is definite and arranged, both work. If it's just an intention, use *be going to*.

---

## Section 147 — Might, Might Not

**MIGHT** — maybe something is or will be true (possibility, 30–50%):
- "Where's Susan?" "She might be in the garden."
- They might win the competition this year.

**MIGHT** — maybe someone will do something:
- I might go for a run this afternoon.
- She might come to the conference.

**MIGHT NOT** — maybe NOT true / maybe will not happen:
- The sky is clearing up. It might not rain this afternoon.
- He might not find another great job.
- I might not come to the party.

**Same form for all subjects:** I, you, he, she, it, we, they + might / might not + infinitive.

---

## Section 148 — Used To / Didn't Use To (review + Usually)

**USED TO + infinitive — past habits or past states no longer true.**

**Form:**
- (+) I/you/etc. used to do.
- (–) I/you/etc. didn't use to do.
- (?) Did I/you/etc. use to do?
- Short answer: Yes, I did. / No, I didn't.

**USED TO vs USUALLY:** USED TO is past only. For present habits use USUALLY.
- ❌ We use to eat out on Saturday. → ✅ We usually eat out on Saturday.

---

## Section 149 — Present Tenses Overview (Simple, Continuous, Perfect)

**Present Simple** (signal words: always, never, usually, often, once a week, etc.):
- Habits / regular actions: I wash my hair every day. / She works as a nurse.
- States / permanent truths: I don't drink coffee. / Water boils at 100 degrees.
- Future in conditional / time clauses: I'll call you as soon as I arrive. / When I am 67, I will retire.

**Present Continuous** (signal words: now, these days, this week, at present, Look!, Listen!):
- Actions in progress: I'm watching a new series now.
- Temporary actions: My car is in the garage; I'm taking the bus this week.
- Future arrangements: I'm seeing the dentist at 6. / We are getting married next week.

**Present Perfect** (signal words: recently, lately, ever, never, just, already, yet, for, since, how long):
- Recent past events relevant now: I've passed the exam! / Have you finished yet?
- Past experiences: Have you ever been to Ireland? / This is the best film I've ever seen.
- Started in the past, still continuing: We've known each other since we were children. / I've lived here for over 20 years.

---

## Section 150 — Past Tenses Overview + Will / Be Going To

**Past Simple** (signal words: yesterday, last week, in 2010, ago, always, often):
- Finished past actions: We arrived last Friday. / I missed the train.
- Chronological order in a story: I opened the door, walked into the room, and saw him.
- Past habits / states: Sarah always arrived on time. / She was very tall.

**Past Continuous** (signal words: while, when, at three o'clock yesterday):
- In-progress past actions: At 10 p.m. I was sleeping. / When she arrived, we were cooking dinner.
- Setting the scene: It was getting dark, and I was walking fast to the pub when…

**WILL** — predictions (we think), instant decisions, promises, suggestions (Shall…?), offers (I'll…):
- I think he'll win the election.
- "We don't have sugar." "Don't worry, I'll ask our neighbour."
- I will always love you.
- Shall we go to the cinema? / I'll carry that bag for you.

**BE GOING TO** — predictions with present evidence; decided plans/intentions:
- I don't know the answers; I'm going to fail the test.
- "Why are you undressing?" "Because I'm going to go for a swim."

---

## Section 151 — Present Perfect vs Past Simple

**Present Perfect** — don't say WHEN (relevance to now):
- I've finished my essay. (It's done now.)
- He has left. (He's not here now.)
- We mention a past experience or recent event with no specific time: She has won two gold medals. / I've broken my arm.
- Unfinished time: He has lived in Japan for years. (He still lives there.) / How long have you been married? (You still are.)

**Past Simple** — say or ask WHEN (finished time):
- When did you finish your essay? / He left after the game. / I didn't go to work yesterday.
- We give details about a past event: "I've broken my arm." "How did it happen?" "I fell in the park."
- Finished time: He lived in Japan for years. (He doesn't live there now.) / How long were you married? (You aren't anymore.)

---

## Section 152 — This vs It

**THIS:**
- Something near you: This cat is cute.
- Introducing someone (in person or on the phone): This is my brother John. / Hello. This is Linda.
- Referring to something just mentioned: This is my house. I bought it last year.

**IT:**
- Times, dates, weather, distance: It's Tuesday and it's very late. / It's not very far from here.
- + adjective + to-infinitive / that-clause: It's nice to see you again. / It's important that you learn this.
- Referring back to something already mentioned: This cat is cute. It is sleeping.

---

## Section 153 — A, THE, No Article (extended)

**A / AN:**
- First mention: I saw an old woman with a dog.
- Jobs / descriptions: Paula is a teacher.
- Meaning "one": Can I have an orange?
- Frequency / rates / speeds: I work 7 hours a day. / It's $500 a month.
- After "what a" / "such a": What a great idea! / It's such a nice place.

**THE:**
- Already known / mentioned: The kids are in the garden.
- We specify which: I sat on the chair in the corner.
- Only one of its kind: The moon is huge.
- Common places in town: I'm going to the bank. / I'm at the library.
- Superlatives: This is the best restaurant in town.
- Oceans, seas, rivers, canals: the Mediterranean, the Nile, the Suez Canal.

**NO ARTICLE:**
- Talk about something in general: I love music. / Mushrooms are delicious.
- home, work, school, bed, hospital, prison (after to/at/in): I go to bed. / You should be at school.
- Meals: Dinner is ready. / Let's have breakfast.
- Days, months, years: Friday is the best day. / I met you in 2020.
- Noun + number: He's in room 15. / Go to page 105.
- TV (meaning programming): I saw it on TV. / I don't watch TV.
- Next / last + time expression: It's next Monday. / I saw him last week.
- Most continents, countries, cities, mountains: Asia, Spain, Rome, Mount Everest.

---

## Section 154 — Another, Other, Others

**ANOTHER + singular noun** (one more / a different one):
- I need another cup of coffee.

**OTHER + plural noun** (more / different ones):
- I want to talk to other doctors. (NOT: others doctors)

**THE OTHER + singular noun** (the second one of two):
- This TV is new. The other TV is old.

**THE OTHER + plural noun** (the remaining ones):
- Where are the other kids? (NOT: the others kids)

**Without a noun:**
- **ANOTHER** = one more: I had one beer, but I'll have another.
- **OTHERS** = different ones: Some people have tea, while others prefer coffee.
- **THE OTHER** = the second of two: This TV is new, but the other is old.
- **THE OTHERS** = the remaining (two or more): Joel and Karen are here, but where are the others?

---

## Section 155 — Can / Could / Be Able To

**Possibility / requests / permission:**
- You can win this game. / You can't smoke here.
- Can I come in? / Can you help me, please?
- Could you speak slowly? (polite)

**Ability — CAN / COULD (general, no other verb forms):**
- He can speak 5 languages.
- I could read at four years old.

**Ability — BE ABLE TO (all other forms — infinitive, future, present perfect):**
- I'd like to be able to be with you. (NOT: I'd like to can…)
- I won't be able to be there. (NOT: I won't can…)
- I haven't been able to study. (NOT: I haven't can…)

**One past occasion (positive)** — use *was/were able to* or *managed to*, NOT *could*:
- ❌ They could escape the fire. → ✅ They were able to / managed to escape the fire.
- BUT negative is fine: They couldn't escape the fire. ✅

---

## Section 156 — Reflexive & Reciprocal Pronouns

**Reflexive pronouns:** myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, yourselves, themselves.

**Use 1 — same subject and object** (action falls on the subject):
- She cut herself chopping the onions.
- You should be really proud of yourself.

**Use 2 — emphasis** (after the subject or at the end):
- Tom finished it himself. / Tom himself finished it.

**BY MYSELF / ON MY OWN** = alone, without anybody else / help:
- She was all day by herself / on her own.
- Tom finished it by himself / on his own.

**Reciprocal pronouns — each other / one another** (A does something to B, and B does something to A):
- You should try to understand one another / each other.

## Section 157 — -ED / -ING Adjectives

**-ING** describes the THING that causes the feeling.
**-ED** describes how the PERSON feels.

| Verb | -ing (inspires the feeling) | -ed (I feel this way) |
|---|---|---|
| annoy | annoying | annoyed |
| astonish | astonishing | astonished |
| bore | boring | bored |
| depress | depressing | depressed |
| devastate | devastating | devastated |
| disappoint | disappointing | disappointed |
| embarrass | embarrassing | embarrassed |
| excite | exciting | excited |
| frighten | frightening | frightened |
| interest | interesting | interested |
| irritate | irritating | irritated |
| surprise | surprising | surprised |
| terrify | terrifying | terrified |
| tire | tiring | tired |
| worry | worrying | worried |

Examples: The film was boring. / I was bored watching the film.

## Section 158 — Active vs Passive Voice

**Active:** Subject (doer) + verb + object (receiver).
- We cleaned the room yesterday.

**Passive:** Subject (receiver) + BE + past participle (+ by + doer).
- The room was cleaned yesterday.

**When to use the passive:**
- The doer is unknown: A bank was robbed yesterday.
- The doer is obvious: The robber was arrested last night.
- You don't want to say who: I was told that you insulted my brother.
- Focus on the receiver, not the doer: Jurassic Park was filmed by Spielberg in 1993.

**Examples:**
- "I will do it now." → He said he would do it then.
- "I have to do it today." → She told me she had to do it that day.
- "Call me tomorrow." → He told me to call him the next day.
- "I arrived yesterday." → She told me she'd arrived the day before.
- "Tom is here." → She said Tom was there.

---

## Section 161 — Reported Speech: Questions and Imperatives

**Wh- questions** — use the question word + statement word order (no auxiliary inversion):
- "When did they arrive?" → He asked me when they had arrived.

**Yes/No questions** — use *if* or *whether* + statement order:
- "Did they call?" → He asked me if / whether they had called.

**Positive imperative — asked/told + me + to-infinitive:**
- "Give me the money." → He told me to give him the money.

**Negative imperative — asked/told + me + not + to-infinitive:**
- "Don't answer the door." → He asked me not to answer the door.

## Section 162 — Quantifiers (summary)

| Quantity | Countable | Uncountable |
|---|---|---|
| Large (positive) | a lot of / lots of | a lot of / lots of |
| Large (neg./?) | many | much |
| Some (positive) | some | some |
| Some (neg./?) | any | any |
| Small (some) | a few | a little / a bit of |
| Almost none | few | little |
| Zero | no / none | no / none |

**Notes:**
- Use *a lot / lots* (no *of*) at the end of a sentence: They made cookies, and I had lots / a lot. / I like her a lot.
- Use SOME in offers / requests: Would you like some tea?
- NONE is not followed by a noun: You have many friends, but I have none.

## Section 166 — Had Better

**HAD BETTER + infinitive** (without *to*) = "someone should do something; it's important". Often a warning.

**Form:** had better = 'd better. Negative: had better not + infinitive.
- It's freezing. You'd better wear a coat.
- We'd better leave now if we want to catch the train.
- You'd better not say a word about this.
- I'd better not forget to lock the door.

**Meaning:** present or future, NOT past. The warning implies something bad might happen if not done.

## Section 167 — It's Time

**IT'S TIME + to-infinitive** — someone should do something now/in the future:
- It's time to go to bed.
- It's time for you to go to the doctor. (add for + person)

**IT'S TIME + subject + past simple** (subjunctive — to talk about the present, often to complain/criticise):
- It's time you went to the doctor.
- It's time you paid me what you owe me. (= you should already have done this)

## Section 168 — Indirect Questions

Used for politeness. The word order becomes statement order (no auxiliary inversion).

**Introduce with:** Could you tell me… / I'm wondering… / I don't know… / I'd like to know…

**Direct:** What time will he come?
**Indirect:** Could you tell me what time he will come?

**Direct:** Will Tom come?
**Indirect:** I'm wondering if / whether Tom will come.

Use **IF** or **WHETHER** when there is no question word (yes/no questions).

## Section 169 — So & Neither (combined review)

**SO + auxiliary + subject** (positive agreement):
- I am tired. → So am I.
- I was at the office. → So was I.
- Sally has finished. → So has Rachel.
- I'll be at the meeting. → So will Bill.
- I'd like to go. → So would I.
- I went to bed late. → So did I.
- Tom can dance. → So can Mary.
- You should sleep more. → So should you.

**NEITHER + auxiliary + subject** (negative agreement):
- I am not tired. → Neither am I.
- I don't like studying. → Neither do I.
- Ed doesn't like music. → Neither does Mary.
- I wasn't ready. → Neither was I.
- You weren't the best. → Neither were you.
- I didn't go. → Neither did I.
- Sally hasn't called yet. → Neither has Bill.
- I won't be at the office. → Neither will I.
- Tom can't speak French. → Neither can Mary.
- You shouldn't be here. → Neither should you.

## Section 170 — The… The… Comparatives (Cause / Effect)

**Structure:** The + comparative + subject + verb, the + comparative + subject + verb.

- The healthier I eat, the better I feel. (= If I eat healthier, I feel better.)
- The harder he works, the more stressed he is.
- The more you do, the more confident you get.
- The less sleep you have, the more tired you get.
- The more you read, the more you know.
- The more you know, the more you need to learn.

**Short forms** (with shorter, common comparatives):
- Call me soon. The sooner, the better. (= The sooner you call, the better it will be.)
- I need a big fridge. The bigger, the better.

## Section 171 — Adjectives Without Noun (referring to groups of people)

**THE + adjective** = a group of people in general.
- She attended a school for the young.
- The young are losing faith in democracy.
- The blind always want more.
- He works for a charity that helps the deaf.
- The old need help.
- The rich often vote conservative.

**THE + nationality adjective (ending in -ch, -sh, -ese, -ss):**
- The English love tea.
- The Swiss make great watches.
- The Chinese invented paper.
- The French enjoy wine during meals.

**THE + nationality plural (for other nationalities):**
- The Brazilians have a great Carnival.
- The Italians are known for their pasta.
- The Greeks created democracy.

## Section 172 — Order of Adjectives

When using multiple adjectives before a noun, follow this order:

**Opinion → Size → Age → Shape → Colour → Origin → Material → Purpose → Noun**

Examples:
- a stupid Spanish man
- a great modern steel kitchen top
- a red French dress (NOT: French red)
- some old wooden tables

Before the adjective string you can put:
- **Determiner:** a, the, my, some.
- **Number / quantity:** two, seven, many, few.

## Section 173 — All Narrative Tenses (extended)

**Past Simple** — completed past actions, past habits/states, duration finished, events in chronological order:
- We went to the zoo last week. / She opened the door, looked at us, and walked into her room.

**Past Continuous** — actions in progress at a past moment / setting the scene:
- At 10 p.m. I was sleeping. / It was getting dark, and I was walking to the pub when…

**Past Perfect Simple** — events that happened earlier in the past / how much/many we had done / duration up to a past point with stative verbs:
- When I met her, I had never been in a serious relationship.
- He noticed that I had cleaned the car.
- We had driven 500 miles and were tired.
- When she died, they had been married for 48 years.

**Past Perfect Continuous** — duration up to a past point with dynamic verbs / repeated actions from earlier in the past:
- I was furious. I had been waiting for him for an hour.
- We had been driving for less than an hour when the car broke down.
- She had been writing a letter every day for over a year.

## Section 174 — May vs Might

Both express possibility (≈ 30–50%). MAY is slightly more formal; MIGHT is more common.

**Possibility (future):**
- They may/might win the match.
- We may/might not arrive on time.

**Possibility (someone will / won't do something):**
- I may/might come with you.
- I may/might not go on holiday.

**Present speculation:**
- They may/might be brothers.
- He may/might not have a job.

**MAY for permission** (more formal):
- You may sit here.
- You may not use your phone.
- May I come in?

---

## Section 175 — Modifying Comparatives

**(Almost) no difference:** as + adj + as / just as / nearly as.
- His second film is **as good as** the first one.
- This is **just as good as** the best pizza in town.

**Small difference:** a bit / a little / slightly + comparative.
- The coffee is **a bit colder than** I prefer.

**Big difference:** much / a lot / far + comparative.
- He's **much taller than** his brother.
- This is **far more interesting** than the other book.

---

## Section 176 — Used To vs Would

Both can describe **repeated past actions** that are no longer true.

**USED TO** — for repeated actions AND past states:
- I used to ski every winter. (repeated action ✓)
- As a child, I used to be blond. (state ✓)

**WOULD** — for repeated actions ONLY, NOT states:
- ✅ When I was a child, we would go shopping every weekend. (repeated action)
- ❌ As a child, I would be blond. → ✅ As a child, I used to be blond. (state — can't use *would*)

Common verbs you can use with WOULD: would go, would play, would visit, would tell, etc.

---

## Section 177 — Pretty, Rather, Quite, Fairly

All four mean roughly "to a moderate / large degree, but less than 'very'". Differences:

**PRETTY** (informal) — less than very, but to a large degree.
- The meal was pretty good. / She cooks pretty well.

**RATHER** (more formal) — less than very, often with negative ideas; with positive ideas if surprising/unexpected.
- The lecture was rather boring.
- His work is rather impressive.
- The dish was rather bland.
- **RATHER A + noun** (uncommon): We had rather a dull morning. (or: a rather dull morning)
- With thoughts/opinions: I rather think you should go.

**QUITE** — meaning depends on the adjective:
- With gradable adjective: "not very, but to a large degree" — The soup was quite good.
- With non-gradable adjective: "completely, absolutely" — The experience was quite incredible.
- Matt swims quite well. / I quite like his new album.
- It's quite a big company. / Quite a few people turned up.

**FAIRLY** — not very, but to a moderate or acceptable degree (weakest).
- The food was fairly good. / The exam was fairly easy.

## Section 178 — HAVE: All Uses & Meanings

**HAVE = possess / suffer (pain, illness):**
- Do you have time? / I have a terrible headache.

**HAVE = take / give / experience / enjoy:**
- How many coffees do you have every day?
- We are having a party next week. Will you come?
- Did you have any trouble finding the place?
- We had so much fun!
- We had a holiday in Los Angeles.

**HAVE TO** for obligation / **DON'T HAVE TO** for absence of necessity:
- I have to wear a uniform at school.
- I didn't have to say anything, because they already knew.
- We don't have to get up too early tomorrow.

**HAVE SOMETHING DONE** = employ someone to do it for you:
- I have my house cleaned every Thursday.
- Are you having your house redecorated?
- Where did you get/have your car repaired?

**HAVE GOT** = have (only present tense — for possession):
- I haven't got any brothers or sisters.
- How many computers have you got?

**HAVE GOT TO** = informal "must" (only for specific obligations):
- I've got to call my boss after lunch.

**HAVE + been** (perfect tenses):
- Have you ever been to Paris?
- She has never been there before.
- By the end of January, I will have been here for two years.

**TO HAVE DONE** (perfect infinitive):
- I'm glad to have met you.
- He pretended to have lost my key.

**Modal + HAVE DONE** (perfect modals):
- You can't have seen Tom. He is in Chicago.
- I must have made a mistake.

## Section 179 — Clauses of Contrast (extended)

**ALTHOUGH / EVEN THOUGH + subject + verb** (no comma after, can begin or be in middle):
- Although we played well, we lost.
- We played well, although we lost.

**HOWEVER / NEVERTHELESS** — semicolon or period before, comma after:
- We played well. However, we lost.
- I eat better. Nevertheless, I keep gaining weight.

**(,) AND + YET + subject + verb:**
- She was tired, and yet she continued to work.
- The job was hard, and yet they didn't complain.

**IN SPITE OF / DESPITE + noun / -ing:**
- We went out in spite of / despite the rain.
- We went out in spite of / despite the fact that it was raining.

**WHEREAS / WHILE + subject + verb** (used for direct contrast, beginning or middle):
- Whereas / While I love cooking, he prefers to eat out.
- John is tall, whereas / while his brother is very short.

---

## Section 180 — Clauses of Purpose (extended)

**TO + infinitive:**
- The new student worked hard to pass the test.

**IN ORDER TO / SO AS TO + infinitive** (more formal):
- I wanted to stay in order to finish the report.
- He left home early so as not to be late.

**IN ORDER NOT TO / SO AS NOT TO + infinitive** (negative purpose):
- Use a plastic hammer in order not to / so as not to damage it.
- They walked quietly so as not to wake up the kids.

**SO (THAT) + subject + verb:**
- We left early so that we could park near the centre.

**FOR + noun** (purpose of an activity):
- We went to the bar for a drink.

**FOR + -ing verb** (function of a thing):
- This knife is used for cutting plastic.

**IN CASE + subject + verb** (precaution):
- Take an umbrella in case it rains.

---

## Section 181 — Clauses of Reason

**BECAUSE + subject + verb:**
- We didn't go because it was raining heavily.
- Because the event was cancelled, I lost my deposit.

**AS / SINCE + subject + verb** (more formal):
- As/Since the roads were blocked, the victims had to be rescued by helicopter.
- We must stay indoors as/since more rain is forecast.

**BECAUSE OF + noun:**
- The concert was postponed because of the heavy rain.

**DUE TO / OWING TO + noun** (more formal):
- The event was cancelled due to / owing to lack of interest.

**ON ACCOUNT OF + noun** (more formal):
- I couldn't enjoy the meal on account of their constant arguing.

---

## Section 182 — Clauses of Result

**SO + subject + verb** (after a comma, in the middle):
- We didn't have anything to do, so we decided to rent a film.

**FOR THIS REASON, + sentence** (at the beginning of a sentence):
- Rent is very expensive in Boston. For this reason, we decided to move to Ohio.

**AS A RESULT, + sentence** (at the beginning):
- The flight was delayed due to the storm. As a result, many passengers complained.

**CONSEQUENTLY / THEREFORE, + sentence** (beginning, or mid position):
- He was a real asset to the company. Consequently, they decided to promote him.
- He is a real asset to the company. They have, therefore, decided to promote him.

---

## Section 183 — Say vs Tell

**TELL + person + something** (TELL takes a direct object — the person):
- ✅ I always tell him I love him.
- ❌ I always say him I love him.
- ✅ The teacher told us to leave.
- ❌ The teacher said us to leave.

**Special TELL expressions** (no "to + person" needed): tell a story, tell the time, tell a lie, tell the truth, tell a secret, tell a joke, tell the difference.
- You must tell the truth. / You must tell us the truth.

**SAY + something** (SAY does NOT need a person, or uses *to*):
- ✅ She said she was happy.
- ✅ She said (that) she was happy.
- ✅ She said something to me.
- ❌ She said me she was happy.

---

## Section 184 — Generic Pronouns

Used to talk about people in general (not specific individuals).

**YOU** — informal, friendly, all-inclusive (very common):
- You never know what the future holds.

**ONE** — more formal:
- One never knows what the future holds.

**WE** — includes the speaker and the listener / community:
- We must look after our planet.

**THEY** — when "they" refers to people in authority or in general (not specific):
- They say it's going to rain tomorrow.
- They've raised the speed limit.

**Passive** — also a way to avoid naming a specific subject:
- It is said that the company is in trouble.

---

## Section 185 — Reflexive / Reciprocal Pronouns (extended)

**Same subject and object (reflexive):**
- She cut herself chopping the onions.
- You should be really proud of yourself.

**To emphasise who did something:**
- End of the sentence: Tom finished it himself.
- After the subject: Tom himself finished it.

**After a preposition:**
- Talking to oneself is not uncommon.

**Exception:** with prepositions of place or *with* meaning "accompanied by", use a regular object pronoun:
- He put their luggage behind him. (NOT himself)
- He took his son with him. (NOT himself)

**BY MYSELF / ON MY OWN** = alone / without help:
- She was all day by herself / on her own.

**Reciprocal pronouns — each other / one another** (A does X to B and B does X to A):
- You should try to understand one another / each other.

---

## Section 186 — There vs It (extended)

**THERE + BE + noun** = something exists somewhere:
- There will be a storm on Saturday.
- There has been some tension.
- There's going to be a conflict.

**THERE + SEEMS / TENDS / IS LIKELY / IS SURE / USED TO + TO BE:**
- There seems to be no possible solution.
- There tends to be some improvement.
- There is sure to be a great vibe.
- There used to be a library here.

**THERE IS NO USE / POINT (IN) + -ing:**
- There is no point in hating anyone.
- There's no use in trying to understand.

**THERE IS NO NEED + to-infinitive:**
- There is no need to panic.

**IT** for time / weather / temperature / distance:
- I have to go. It's 5 o'clock.
- It's Saturday, and it is raining.
- It's only two miles from the hotel.

**IT** to refer to something just mentioned:
- I like your new song. It sounds great!

**IT + be / seem + adjective + to-infinitive:**
- It's been hard to find them.
- It seems impossible to get out of here.

**IT + be + adjective / noun + that-clause:**
- It's strange that they aren't here yet.
- It's a shame that you can't come.

**IT TAKES + (someone) + time + to-infinitive:**
- It takes me 20 minutes to get to work.

**Cleft sentences:**
- It was Peter who took your car.
- It's my laptop that doesn't work.

## Section 187 — Meanings of GET (extended)

**GET + noun:**
- Receive: We get lots of emails every day.
- Bring: Can you get me a glass of water?
- Take (transport): We get the bus to go to school.
- Obtain: He got his driving license last week.
- Buy: I'll go to the grocery store to get some milk.

**GET TO + place** (arrive / reach):
- We got to London on the 8 o'clock train.
- How long does it take you to get to work?

**GET + adjective / past participle** (become / start to be):
- He gets angry very easily.
- It's very late, and they're not here. I'm getting worried.

**GET as passive auxiliary** (informal alternative to BE):
- She almost got killed by a drunk driver.
- His articles are always getting criticised by the press.

**GET something done** (= have something done):
- We are getting the house painted next week.
- You should get your tyres changed.

**GET someone to do something** (= make/ask/persuade someone):
- How can parents get their children to read more?
- I don't know how to get my partner to do more things around the house.

## Section 188 — Discourse Markers

Common written / formal connectors and their meanings:

- **BY THE WAY / INCIDENTALLY** — on a different / related topic.
- **ACTUALLY / IN FACT / AS A MATTER OF FACT** — to give a stronger or contrary fact.
- **ANYWAY / ANYHOW / IN ANY CASE** — returning to the main point.
- **AS I WAS SAYING** — to continue from where you left off.
- **HENCE / THUS** — because / for this reason.
- **DESPITE / IN SPITE OF** — despite what is believed.
- **FORTUNATELY / LUCKILY** — on a positive note.
- **AT LEAST** — at a / the minimum.
- **OVERALL / ALL IN ALL** — considering everything.
- **IN GENERAL / GENERALLY** — usually / on average.
- **MOREOVER / FURTHERMORE / ADDITIONALLY** — adding information.
- **OBVIOUSLY / CLEARLY** — making something explicit.
- **BASICALLY** — essentially / in essence.
- **IN OTHER WORDS** — to rephrase.
- **OTHERWISE** — if not.
- **REGARDING / AS REGARDS / AS FAR AS … IS CONCERNED** — relating to a topic.
- **ON THE OTHER HAND / CONVERSELY / IN CONTRAST** — to contrast.
- **WHILE / WHEREAS** — also for direct contrast.

## Section 189 — Past Modals of Deduction (extended)

**MUST HAVE + past participle / MUST HAVE BEEN + -ing** — sure it WAS true:
- She must have been in the garden when I called.
- I must have been sleeping when the fire started.

**CAN'T / COULDN'T HAVE + past participle / + been + -ing** — sure it was NOT true:
- You can't / couldn't have seen her. She was at home.
- She can't / couldn't have been waiting for far.

**MAY / MIGHT (NOT) HAVE + past participle / + been + -ing** — maybe true / not:
- He may / might have taken a different train.
- She didn't answer. She might have been having a shower.
- COULD also possible (could have / could be hurt).

**SHOULD / OUGHT TO HAVE + past participle / + been + -ing** — expected outcome (often regret/criticism):
- He should / ought to have been studying for his exams, but he wasn't.
- You should have packed your things.

## Section 190 — Distancing Expressions (formal / journalism)

**SEEM / APPEAR:**

- **It seems / appears + that-clause:** It seems / appears that the new series will begin shooting soon.
- **It would seem / appear + that-clause:** It would seem / appear that the situation is finally under control.
- **Subject + seem / appear + to-infinitive:** He seems / appears to be very concerned. / They seem / appear to be facing difficulties.
- **There seem(s) to be / to have been:** There seem to be two options for kids. / There seems to have been a great response on social media.

**Other expressions:**
- **APPARENTLY** — Apparently, the new prime minister will not visit Cuba until the end of May.
- **ALLEGEDLY** — He allegedly used a toy gun to rob the store.
- **ACCORDING TO** — According to witnesses, she abandoned the place well after midnight.
- **MAY / MIGHT** for speculation — They might have fled the scene. / They may be spending their Christmas holiday together.

## Section 191 — Verb + Object + To-Infinitive

**Verbs that take this pattern:** advise, allow, ask, beg, cause, convince, enable, encourage, expect, force, get, help, intend, invite, mean, order, persuade, remind, take (time), teach, tell, warn.

- The doctor advised me to change my diet.
- Mike's parents didn't allow him to go to the festival.
- He helped me with his project.

**Verbs that can also take *object + to-infinitive*:** want, need, would like, would hate, would prefer.
- I want you to do something for me.
- I'd hate you to think I didn't appreciate what you did for me.

**Some verbs (advise, persuade, remind, teach, tell, warn) also take + that-clause:**
- He advised us that we should arrive early. (OR: He advised us to arrive early.)

**Some verbs (advise, allow) can take + -ing:**
- He advised going. (OR: He advised me to go.)
- They don't allow drinking. (OR: They don't allow me to drink.)

---

## Section 192 — Permission, Possibility, Obligation

**BE ABLE TO / BE ALLOWED TO** — permission / possibility:
- We were able to / allowed to eat all we wanted.
- You will be able to finish in time.
- Note: ALLOWED is not common after IT IS — say "We are allowed to use mobile phones" or "Using mobile phones is allowed".

**BE PERMITTED TO** — permission/prohibition in formal/official contexts:
- It is permitted to take photos of the archive documents.
- Picnics are not permitted in the park.
- Employees are permitted to use mobile devices.

**BE SUPPOSED TO / BE MEANT TO** — what should or shouldn't be done:
- We are supposed to / meant to check in one hour before take-off.
- You aren't supposed to / meant to be here.

**HAD BETTER / HAD BETTER NOT** — advice with a warning:
- We'd better hurry up, or we'll miss our train.
- You'd better not tell her you broke the vase — she'll get very mad.

---

## Section 193 — FOR + someone + To-Infinitive

Used to specify WHO should/could do the action with an infinitive structure.

**After verbs:**
- I will arrange for you to have a meeting with him next week.
- I am waiting for the technician to come and fix my air conditioner.

**After adjectives:**
- It's important for us to be ready when we are needed.
- It would be impossible for them to be there on the day of the rehearsal.

**After nouns:** advantage, disadvantage, demand, disaster, idea, mistake, etc.
- It was a mistake for me to lend him the money.
- It would be a disaster for us to increase the number of staff.

**With too / enough:**
- The temptation was too great for me to forget.
- It was warm enough for us to sleep in the open.

**Infinitive of purpose:**
- This activity is for kids to develop their listening skills.
- The goal is for the dog to stay calm when wearing a leash.

---

## Section 194 — Verb + Object + Infinitive Without TO

**LET / MAKE / HELP:**
- She drives me to work and never lets me pay for the petrol.
- Can I help you paint the fence? (also: help you to paint)
- The teacher made us write a very long essay.

**BE MADE TO + to-infinitive** (passive — needs *to*):
- We were made to write a long essay.

**HEAR / LISTEN / NOTICE / SEE + object + infinitive (no *to*)** — short / complete actions:
- I saw him kiss her. (short action, start to end)
- I heard someone shout your name.

**HEAR / LISTEN / NOTICE / SEE + object + -ing** — longer actions in progress:
- I saw them kissing in the park.

---

## Section 195 — Conditionals: IF Alternatives

**UNLESS** = if not:
- I won't go to the party unless you go too. (= if you don't go too)

**IN CASE** = because something might happen (precaution):
- I'll take an umbrella **if** it rains. (only if it rains)
- I'll take an umbrella **in case** it rains. (because it might rain)

**WHETHER OR NOT** = something will happen in either possibility:
- I'll help him whether or not he needs me.

**EVEN IF** = whether or not (more emphasis):
- Even if you apologise, he'll never forgive you.

**PROVIDED THAT / AS LONG AS / ON CONDITION THAT** = if (emphasising the condition):
- I'll tell you what really happened provided that / as long as you can keep the secret.
- I'll lend you the money on condition that you pay me back.

**SUPPOSE / SUPPOSING** = if (imagining a situation):
- Supposing I got a job, I wouldn't be able to travel with you next summer.
- Suppose she doesn't believe you — what would you do then?

---

## Section 196 — Future: Plans & Predictions (consolidated)

**Intentions, plans, decisions:**
- **WILL** — instant decisions: "Don't worry, I'll buy some."
- **BE GOING TO** — intentions; time/place may not be decided: I'm leaving early tomorrow.
- **PRESENT CONTINUOUS** — definite plans with time/place decided: We're getting married next week.
- **FUTURE CONTINUOUS** — definite plan or polite question about plans: Will you be going home this summer?
- **PRESENT SIMPLE** — scheduled / timetabled events: The train leaves at 4.

**Predictions:**
- **WILL** — we think something will happen: He will be a good doctor.
- **BE GOING TO** — we see evidence: Don't drive like crazy. We're going to have an accident!

---

## Section 197 — Future Perfect Simple & Continuous

**Future Perfect Simple** = WILL HAVE + past participle. Actions finished before a future time:
- By this time tomorrow, I will have finished my work.

Also: duration up to a future time (with stative verbs):
- In 10 years' time, I will have been married for 20 years.

**Future Perfect Continuous** = WILL HAVE BEEN + -ing. Duration of an action up to a future time (with dynamic verbs):
- In 10 years' time, I will have been working here for 20 years.

**Time expressions:** by tomorrow / next week / 2050, by this time tomorrow, by the time + subject + verb, in two weeks' time, when, before.

---

## Section 198 — Probability: Likely / Unlikely / Bound / Sure

**BE BOUND TO / BE SURE TO + infinitive** — certain or very likely to happen:
- He's bound / sure to win the final.
- The film is bound / sure to be a hit.

**THERE IS / ARE BOUND / SURE + to be:**
- There are bound / sure to be problems.

**LIKELY / UNLIKELY** — probably will / will not happen:
- You will likely get the job.
- He's likely our best player.

**Subject + BE (UN)LIKELY + to-infinitive:**
- He is likely to win the game.
- They are unlikely to find anything.

**IT'S (UN)LIKELY + that-clause:**
- It's likely that he will win the game.
- It's unlikely that they will find out.

**Common mistake:**
- ❌ He will unlikely win the game.
- ✅ He is unlikely to win the game. / It's unlikely that he will win. / He probably won't win.

---

## Section 199 — Other Ways to Express the Future (extended)

- **BE ABOUT TO + infinitive** — will happen very soon: The new app is about to be launched.
- **BE ON THE BRINK / POINT / VERGE OF + -ing** — will happen very soon: This new technology is on the brink of changing the world.
- **BE DUE TO + infinitive** — planned / expected events: They are due to leave the country next week.
- **BE TO + infinitive** — official arrangements / orders / formal: The president is to sign the agreement. / All employees are to attend the orientation. / Used in if-clauses to describe a desired result: If he is to succeed, he will need to learn many things.
- **BE (UN)LIKELY TO** — probability: He is likely to win.
- **BE BOUND TO + infinitive** — near-certainty: His new film is bound to win the hearts of every romantic out there.

---

## Section 200 — Ellipsis: Omitting Words

We can omit repeated parts of a sentence when the meaning is clear.

**Omit a repeated subject / subject + auxiliary** after AND, BUT, OR:
- I closed the door and (I) took off his shoes.
- We could go out or (we could) have a party at home.

**Omit a repeated adjective / adverb:**
- They say he is the best right now, but I don't think he is (the best).
- Although it seems far, it isn't (far).

**Omit a repeated verb phrase after the same auxiliary / modal:**
- Maria should take the exam, but I don't think you should (take the exam).

**Omit after a different auxiliary / modal:**
- I'm studying for the exams but not as much as I should (be studying).
- She didn't win, but she could have (won).

**With DO / DOES / DID when there is no auxiliary:**
- She likes it, but I don't (like it).

**After TO-(infinitive):**
- I shouldn't go out tonight, but I really want to (go out tonight).
- Are you going to sell the car? — No, I've decided not to (sell the car).

---

## Section 201 — Substitution: Replacing Words

**ONE / ONES** — replaces singular / plural noun:
- "Which car do you prefer?" "I like the red one."
- Will you wear these trousers or the ones that I gave you?

**DO SO** — replaces a verb phrase:
- Don't apologise because doing so would be admitting you were wrong.

**SO / NOT** — replaces a clause after speaking/thinking verbs:
- "Are they coming?" "I think so." / "I don't think so."
- "Shall we go for a run tomorrow?" "I'm afraid not."

**SO + auxiliary + subject** — to agree:
- "I can be there at any time tomorrow." "So can I!" (I can be there too.)
- "I shouldn't say anything, and neither should you." (You shouldn't either.)

**Conditional replacement** — use *if so* / *if not* to avoid repeating an entire clause:
- Do you want to be better? If so, pay attention to what I have to say.
- Mr Chen should be there when you arrive. If not, just give me a call.

---

## Section 202 — Cleft Sentences (Emphasis)

Cleft sentences split one statement into two clauses to put emphasis on one part.

**THE PERSON WHO / THE THING THAT / THE PLACE WHERE / THE DAY WHEN / THE REASON WHY + clause + BE + emphasised part:**
- Original: Sheila hid the book under the mattress.
- The person who hid the book under the mattress was Sheila.
- The thing (that) Sheila hid under the mattress was the book.
- The place where Sheila hid the book was under the mattress.
- The day when I have to call is Monday.
- The reason why I have to call on Monday is to schedule an appointment.

**IT + BE + emphasised phrase + WHO / THAT + clause:**
- It was Sheila who hid the book under the mattress.
- It was the book that Sheila hid under the mattress.
- It was under the mattress that Sheila hid the book.

**WHAT + clause + BE + emphasised noun phrase:**
- What Sheila hid under the mattress was the book.
- What I want is some of that cake.

**WHAT + subject + DO / DOES / DID + IS / WAS + (TO-) infinitive** (emphasising an action):
- What Sheila did was (to) hide the book under the mattress.
- What you should do is (to) save some money.

**WHAT HAPPENS / HAPPENED IS / WAS (THAT) + clause** (emphasising a whole sentence):
- What happened was that Sheila hid the book under the mattress.
- What's happening is that he is afraid of losing his investment.

---

## Section 203 — Defining vs Non-Defining Relative Clauses

**DEFINING** (no commas) — information necessary to identify the noun:
- I called my brother who lives in Ontario. (I have several brothers; I called this one.)
- *That* can replace *who/which*: That's the mug that I bought yesterday.
- We can omit *who/which/that* when followed by subject + verb: Do you like the song (that) I sang?

**NON-DEFINING** (between commas) — extra, non-essential information:
- My brother, who lives in Ontario, is older. (I have only one brother.)
- We CANNOT use *that*: These bikes, which cost a fortune, are made in Japan.
- *Who/which* CANNOT be omitted: They introduced me to John, who is a doctor.

---

## Section 204 — Relative Pronouns

**WHO / WHOM* / THAT** — for people:
- As subject: That's the man who/that stole the money.
- As object (can be omitted): We need to find people (who/that/whom) we can trust.
- *WHOM is formal, rarely used in spoken English.

**WHICH / THAT** — for things/animals:
- As subject: That is the dog which/that attacked me.
- As object (can be omitted): I'll wear the trousers (which/that) I bought in Paris.

**WHOSE / OF WHICH** — for possession:
- After possessor: This is the story of a man whose ideas saved lots of lives.
- For things (formal): The film, whose title (= the title of which) is unknown, will be set in the '60s.

---

## Section 205 — Relative Adverbs

**WHEN / AT/IN/ON WHICH / THAT** — for time:
- 1978 was the year **when** he joined the army. (relative adverb)
- 1978 was the year **in which** he joined the army. (preposition + which)
- 1978 was the year **(that)** he joined the army. (informal)

**WHERE / AT/IN/ON WHICH / (THAT)…AT/IN/ON** — for place:
- That's the restaurant **where** we ate last Friday.
- That's the restaurant **at which** we ate last Friday.
- That's the restaurant **(that)** we ate in last Friday. (informal)

**WHY / FOR WHICH / (THAT)** — for reason:
- He didn't explain the reason **why** he did that.
- He didn't explain the reason **for which** he did that. (formal)
- He didn't explain the reason **(that)** he did that. (informal)

---

## Section 206 — Participle Clauses

Adverbial -ING or -ED clauses used to express **purpose, reason, cause, result, or time** more concisely (mainly in written English). The subject of the participle and the main verb must be the same.

**-ING** — for present or past, active actions:
- Walking quickly, I soon caught up with him. (= Because I was walking quickly)
- Talking to you, I always feel better. (= When I talk to you)

**HAVING + past participle** — emphasises that the action happened earlier; often expresses cause:
- Having driven for hours, I was desperate to find a restaurant.
- Having lost three job interviews, he felt he would never find a job.

**-ED / past participle** — replaces a passive clause, often equivalent to a relative clause:
- Found in a litter bin, the briefcase contained classified information. (= which was found)
- Impressed by the painting, John praised the artist. (= who was impressed)

**Same-subject rule:**
- ❌ Mixed with vodka, the cocktail tastes even better. (cocktail isn't mixing itself)
- ✅ When mixed with vodka, the cocktail tastes even better.

---

## Section 207 — Possessive 's with Time Words

We can use the possessive 's with time expressions in two equivalent ways:

| With 's | With OF |
|---|---|
| one hour's drive | a drive of one hour |
| two years' experience | an experience of two years |
| three hours' journey | a journey of three hours |

Examples:
- You need two years' experience to get a job.
- He lives one hour's drive from the airport.
- The three hours' journey to the valley was amazing.

## Section 208 — Noun + Preposition Collocations

Common patterns:

**+ FOR:** appointment for, argument for, contact for, demand for, need for, recipe for, respect for, room for, use for.

**+ IN:** belief in, change in, decrease in, increase in, interest in, rise in, success in.

**+ ON:** agreement on, article on, ban on, debate on, decision on, information on, report on.

**+ TO:** addiction to, attention to, invitation to, promise to, reaction to, solution to, threat to.

**+ OF:** advantage of, cause of, chance of, cost of, lack of, possibility of, photo of, story of.

**+ ABOUT:** argument about, article about, complaint about, concern about, confusion about, conversation about, discussion about.

**+ BETWEEN / WITH:** bond between, connection between, difference between, distance between, gap between, link between, relationship between.

## Section 209 — Never vs Ever

**NEVER** = not ever / at no time / zero times.
- Imperative (command/advice): Never say that again. / Never give up.
- Present simple (how often): I never travel alone. / He never answers my calls.
- Present perfect (zero times): I've never been to Italy. / They've never told you the truth.
- With superlatives: This is the best movie I've ever seen.
- With *before* (first-time experience): I've never seen this man before.

**EVER** = at any time / at all times.
- Negative imperative: Don't ever say that again. / Don't ever give up.
- Present simple questions: Do you ever go swimming?
- Present perfect negatives: She hasn't ever seen a ghost.
- Present perfect questions: Have you ever tried sushi?
- The first / second / etc. time: It's the second time he's ever won a prize.

---

## Section 210 — IF vs WHETHER

**IF** only — in conditional sentences:
- If you study, you will pass the exam.
- I'd be happier if I didn't have to work.

**IF or WHETHER** — both work:
- Before clauses expressing doubt: I'm not sure if / whether he'll be there.
- In indirect yes/no questions: Do you know if / whether John is here yet?

**WHETHER** only:
- Choice between two options: I couldn't decide whether to get the red or the green one.
- Before a to-infinitive: We weren't sure whether to invite him.
- After prepositions: The job offer depends on whether he passes his driving test.
- Before "or not": We are still uncertain as to whether or not the request will be approved.

---

## Section 211 — Instead & Instead Of

**INSTEAD** (alone) = preferably / as an alternative.
- I don't drink coffee. I drink tea instead. (more common, at the end)
- I don't drink coffee. Instead, I drink tea. (after a comma at the beginning)

**INSTEAD OF + noun / pronoun / -ing** = in place of.
- I drink tea instead of coffee.
- Nowadays, I drink red wine instead of beer.
- I'm staying with John instead of Mary.
- I'll use yours instead of mine.
- Instead of eating indoors, we decided to eat in the garden.

---

## Section 212 — Bring vs Take

**BRING** = carry/move something **to** the speaker's location:
- Can you bring me a glass of water?
- She brought us souvenirs from her trip.

**BRING** to the listener's location:
- What did Lucy bring you from Rome?
- Wait here. I'll come at 7, and I'll bring Dave.

**TAKE** = carry/move something **away** from the speaker's or listener's location:
- Can you take me to the station?
- Make sure you take your umbrella when you go to work.

When neither speaker nor listener is involved, choose BRING or TAKE based on the point of view:
- Sandra always brings him to visit her parents. (from her parents' point of view)
- Sandra always takes him to visit her parents. (from Sandra's point of view)

---

## Section 213 — Remember / Forget: To Do vs Doing

**REMEMBER TO + infinitive** — things we need to do (future obligation):
- He didn't remember to lock the door when he left.
- I must remember to pay the electricity bill.

**REMEMBER + -ing** — recall memories of past actions:
- I remember coming to this place as a child.
- Do you remember eating at this restaurant before?

**FORGET TO + infinitive** — things we need to do but don't remember:
- I think I forgot to lock the door when we left.
- Don't forget to call when you finish. (reminder)

**FORGET + -ing** — past actions/memories we won't forget:
- I'll never forget swimming in this river for the first time.
- How can you forget meeting him at the party?

---

## Section 214 — While vs Whereas

**WHILE** — less formal; can mean contrast OR simultaneous action OR a period of time.

**Contrast** (subject + verb):
- While Tom's eyes are blue, his sister's are brown.
- Mary likes coffee, while John prefers tea.

**Simultaneous actions** (more common):
- While I was driving, it started to snow.
- I often watch television while I eat dinner.

**Noun (a period of time):**
- I'll be back in a while.
- I haven't seen him in a while.

**WHEREAS** — more formal; only for contrast.
- Whereas the former specimen was toxic, the latter was not.
- The climate in the north is temperate, whereas it is tropical in the south.

---

## Section 215 — Look vs Watch vs See

**LOOK (AT)** — intentional, short.
- Why are you looking at me?
- Have a look at this!

**LOOK + adjective** = seem:
- You look tired today.

**WATCH** — look at something moving / changing over a long period; intentional.
- I sat there watching the trains come and go.
- I'm going to watch the match on television.

**SEE** — non-intentional perception (just happens).
- I can see a man over there.

**SEE** also = understand:
- I see what you mean.

---

## Section 216 — Possessive 's: Spelling

**Singular noun + 's:** my father's car, the dog's toys, the president's suspicions.

**Irregular plural + 's:** a children's story, the men's room, people's problems.

**Regular plural ending in -s + ' (just an apostrophe):** the boys' restroom, the teachers' lounge, tourists' passports.

**Singular noun ending in -s + 's or +':** Chris's (or Chris') computer, Tomas's car, Douglas's house.

**Two or more nouns — share possession:** 's after the last noun → Anna and Tom's car (they share one).

**Two or more nouns — separate possession:** 's after each noun → Anna's and Tom's cars (each has one).

## Section 217 — Clauses of Contrast & Purpose (review)

**Contrast:**
- ALTHOUGH / EVEN THOUGH + subject + verb (beginning or middle, no comma after).
- HOWEVER + comma (with semicolon or period before).
- IN SPITE OF / DESPITE + noun / -ing / the fact that.

**Purpose:**
- TO + infinitive.
- IN ORDER TO / IN ORDER NOT TO + infinitive.
- SO AS TO / SO AS NOT TO + infinitive.
- SO THAT + subject + verb.
- FOR + noun.

(See Sections 119 and 179 for full examples.)

## Section 218 — Between vs Among

**BETWEEN** — in the middle of TWO OR MORE individual entities (you can identify each):
- Alice is standing between John and Silvia.
- They built the monument between the lake, the temple, and the tree.
- The help desk is open between noon and 10 p.m.
- There is a difference between the two products.
- Choose between the chocolate and the vanilla.

**AMONG** — in the middle of a GROUP or mass (not individuals):
- Alice is standing among a group of children.
- They built the monument among the trees.
- There was an agreement among the members.
- The sweets were distributed among the children.
- Sara is among our most promising students.

---

## Section 219 — Little vs A Little, Few vs A Few

**LITTLE / FEW** — negative meaning ("not enough"):
- There's little milk left in the fridge. We should buy some today.
- There were few customers, so we decided to close the shop early.

**A LITTLE / A FEW** — positive meaning ("some / enough"):
- There is a little milk left in the fridge, so we don't need to buy any more today.
- There were a few customers on the first day, so we made some money.

**LITTLE / A LITTLE** + uncountable singular noun.
**FEW / A FEW** + countable plural noun.

---

## Section 220 — Less vs Fewer

**LESS + uncountable / singular noun:**
- I have less money now.
- Sam has less patience than you.

**FEWER + countable plural noun:**
- We had fewer than ten guests.
- There are fewer apples this year.

**Exception** — LESS is used with money / time / weight / distance units, even though they look plural:
- I try to spend less than 50 dollars on food each week.
- It took me less than 10 minutes to clean the floor.
- She weighs less than 100 pounds.
- Gary lives less than 2 miles away from his office.

---

## Section 221 — Lay vs Lie

**LAY** (lay / laid / laid / laying) — to put something down. **Needs an object.**
- I'll lay the paper on the table.
- I laid the baby down for a nap.

**LIE** (lie / lay / lain / lying) — to be or rest in a horizontal position. **No object.**
- I like to lie on my bed and listen to music.
- Yesterday, I lay in the sun for an hour.
- I have lain in bed all morning.

⚠️ The present *lay* (to put) and the past of *lie* (lay) are spelled the same — context decides:
- I laid a towel on the floor and I lay on it.

---

## Section 222 — Raise vs Rise

**RAISE** (raise / raised / raised / raising) — to put something up. **Regular. Needs an object.**
- We raise the flag every morning.
- I raised my hand to answer the question.

**RISE** (rise / rose / risen / rising) — to go up or increase. **Irregular. No object.**
- Prices rise every year.
- Sea levels rose significantly last year.
- The temperature has risen 5°C this week.

---

## Section 223 — Above vs Over

**ABOVE** — higher position, NOT directly over (no contact, no covering):
- The stars were shining above the city.
- They had a cabin on a hill above the forest.

**ABOVE** — higher level or standard / temperature / height / etc.:
- His IQ is above average.
- The water level rose above 2 m.
- The temperature was above 0°C.
- He is above 6 feet tall.

**OVER** — touching or covering:
- She put a blanket over the baby.
- I held the umbrella over my head.

**OVER** — from one side to the other (motion):
- The plane flew over the mountains.
- We had to walk over the bridge.

**OVER** — age / speed / "more than" (informal):
- She is over 30 but looks younger.
- He was driving over 100 km/h.
- They have over 1,000 employees.

---

## Section 224 — On The Other Hand vs On The Contrary

**ON THE OTHER HAND** — introduces a contrasting idea or another perspective (similar to *however*):
- I don't find working out boring. On the other hand, it's hard to stay motivated.
- I don't hate my new job at all. On the other hand, I don't plan to do this for a long time.

**ON THE CONTRARY** — states the OPPOSITE of the previous statement (often after a negative):
- I don't find working out boring. On the contrary, it's a lot of fun!
- I don't hate my new job at all. On the contrary, I absolutely love it.

---

## Section 225 — How to Avoid Double Subjects

A double subject is when the subject is repeated — usually as **noun + pronoun** or **-ing phrase + pronoun**. It's a common mistake.

**Wrong patterns:**
- ❌ My friend she speaks Spanish. → ✅ My friend speaks Spanish.
- ❌ Walking every day it's important. → ✅ Walking every day is important.
- ❌ My dog he barks a lot. → ✅ My dog barks a lot. / He barks a lot.
- ❌ Rosa and I we are married. → ✅ Rosa and I are married.

**Rule:** Use a noun OR a pronoun as the subject, NEVER both.

---

## Section 226 — Amount vs Number vs Quantity

| | AMOUNT OF | NUMBER OF | QUANTITY OF |
|---|---|---|---|
| Used with | Uncountable nouns | Countable plural nouns | Countable and uncountable |
| Tone | Everyday, neutral | Everyday, neutral | Formal, technical, academic |

Often used with size adjectives: large, small, huge, significant.
- She drank a large amount of water after the run.
- A small number of students missed the test.
- Only a small quantity of paper is produced through recycling.

**Notes:** *Number of* often appears without a size adjective. *Quantity of* can replace *amount/number of* in formal/technical English but is uncommon in everyday speech.

---

## Section 227 — Like vs As

**LIKE — preposition + noun / pronoun / -ing:**
- To compare two similar things: She sings like a professional. (She's not a pro, but sings like one.)
- To give examples: I love games like chess and darts.

**AS — preposition + noun = in the role of / in the function of:**
- He works as a teacher.
- He used his backpack as a pillow.

**AS vs LIKE with people:**
- He spoke to me **as** a friend. (He IS my friend — that role.)
- He spoke to me **like** a friend. (He's not my friend but spoke that way.)

**LIKE + subject + verb** — informal (especially American English), not correct in formal British English:
- Nobody knows him like I do.

**AS + subject + verb** — formal:
- Nobody knows him as I do.

**LIKE + in/on/at + …** — common in spoken English; informal:
- Like in 2020, travel restrictions may apply this year.

**AS + in/on/at + …** — correct in formal writing:
- As in 2020, travel restrictions may apply this year.

---

## Section 228 — Clean vs Wash

**CLEAN** — remove dust, dirt, stains; tidy / organise.
- I clean the kitchen every day.
- Can you clean your desk now?
- Things we clean: room, house, window, desk, shoes.
- Adjective: clean = not dirty.

**WASH** — use water (and usually soap) to remove dirt.
- Please wash your hands.
- It's your turn to wash the dishes.
- Things we wash: hands, dishes, car, hair, clothes.
- Noun: a wash → My car needs a wash.

**Compare:**
- Wash the car = use water and soap to make it clean.
- Clean the car = remove rubbish, tidy up, dust, maybe wash too.
- Wash your shoes = put them in water (maybe washing machine).
- Clean your shoes = remove dirt with a brush/cloth, polish them.

---

## Section 229 — Double Negatives

A double negative is two negative words in the same sentence — **not considered correct** in standard English.

**Common negative words:**
- Adverbs: not, never, nowhere, hardly, barely, scarcely, rarely, seldom.
- Pronouns / determiners: no, nobody, no one, nothing, none, neither (… nor).

**Wrong → Right:**
- ❌ I don't know nothing. → ✅ I don't know anything. / I know nothing.
- ❌ She didn't go nowhere. → ✅ She didn't go anywhere. / She went nowhere.
- ❌ I can't hardly breathe. → ✅ I can hardly breathe.

**Rule:** Use *any-* words after a negative, or use ONE negative word, not both.

**When double negatives ARE OK** — when used for soft / polite / less negative effect:
- I didn't not like it. (= I liked it a bit.)
- I can't not help her. (= I must help.)
- It's not impossible. (= It's possible.)

---

## Section 230 — Reduced Infinitives

You can use **TO** alone instead of repeating the full infinitive phrase.

- I haven't watched the film, but I'm going to. (= going to watch the film)
- I don't play tennis now, but I used to. (= used to play tennis)

Keep the full form when the verb is BE:
- I know I'm not very positive, but I really try to be.

You can leave out TO when the meaning is clear:
- She wanted to help, and she really tried (to).
- You can go whenever you want, but there's no need (to).

**Don't leave out TO with:** afford, advise, be able, choose, decide, plan, pretend, would like / love / hate / prefer, want, choose, and negative infinitives (not to):
- We need to buy a new car, but we can't afford to.
- "Will you help me?" "I don't want to."
- I wanted to travel there, but David advised me not to.

After if / when / what / as with want/like, TO is often dropped:
- You can leave if you want (to).
- You can come over when you like. (NOT: when you like to)

## Section 231 — Wish vs Hope vs Expect

| Verb | Main use | Typical structures | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **WISH** | Desire for unlikely / impossible things to be different | wish + past simple / wish + past perfect / wish + would | I wish I had more time. / I wish I had been there. / I wish you'd stop that. |
| **WISH** | Politely say what you want to do (formal) | wish + to-infinitive (= would like) | I wish to speak to the manager. |
| **HOPE** | Desire for something possible / likely in the future | hope + to-infinitive / hope + (that) + subject + present / hope + (that) + can / will | I hope to see you soon. / I hope it doesn't rain tomorrow. / I hope you can / will come to the party. |
| **EXPECT** | You think something will probably happen | expect + to-infinitive / expect (that) + clause / expect + object + to-infinitive | I expect to get my results next week. / I expect (that) it will be a hot summer. / I expect John to finish this by Friday. |

---

## Section 232 — Until vs By

**UNTIL** — shows HOW LONG something continues; introduces when it stops.
- She lived in Paris until 2020.
- Wait here until I come back.
- I will study until 8 o'clock. (continuous through 8)

**BY** — shows WHEN something must happen; deadline (= at or before this time).
- I need to be there by 8 o'clock.
- Send the report by Friday.
- I will finish this task by 8 o'clock. (sometime before, but no later than)

---

## Section 233 — Will, Would (C1 uses)

**WILL — present uses beyond simple future:**
- Characteristic behaviours / habits: Every day, my grandad will tell the same stories.
- Things always true (general truth): Oil will float on water.
- Single occasion — usually only in negative: The car won't start.
- Criticise habits / behaviour: She won't listen to anything you say.
- Assumptions about the present: That'll be Mary. Go and open the door.
- *will have + past participle* — present deduction: She'll have left by now.
- *if you will* — criticism / disapproval: "I'm really tired." "Well, if you will stay up all night, don't complain."

**WOULD — past / hypothetical / polite uses:**
- Past characteristic behaviours, habits, repeated actions: When we were students, we would stay up late talking. / The river would freeze in winter.
- *Wouldn't* — refusal / unwillingness in the past: The car wouldn't start yesterday.
- Criticise past behaviour: She wouldn't listen to anything you said.
- *would have + past participle* — imaginary past situations: I would've helped, but he said no.
- Tactful, polite, tentative: I'd have to agree with you on that. / I would've thought he'd apologise.

---

## Section 234 — Should (C1 uses)

**SHOULD — extended uses:**
- Recommendations / mild obligations: People should treat others with respect.
- Logical conclusions: That should be enough to fix the problem.
- Things expected to be true now / future: He should be home by now. / The results should be ready tomorrow.
- *should have + past participle* — regret / past criticism: They shouldn't have spoken to her like that.

**Formal SHOULD in subordinate clauses:**
- After verbs like suggest, propose, advise + that-clause: I suggest that he should apologise.
- After adjectives of importance / emotion + that-clause: It's essential that we should act quickly.
- In conditional clauses (formal): If you should need any help, just let me know.
- In purpose clauses (formal): Take my number in case you should need anything.

---

## Section 235 — Auxiliary Verbs: DO, BE, HAVE

**DO** — forms negatives and questions in the present and past simple:
- don't / doesn't + infinitive (present simple): I don't like spicy food.
- didn't / did + infinitive (past simple): Did you finish the report?

**BE** — forms continuous tenses and the passive:
- BE + -ing (present continuous): They aren't watching TV.
- BE + -ing (past continuous): I was cooking dinner at 8 o'clock.
- BE + past participle (present passive): Is the homework finished?
- BE + past participle (past passive): The emails weren't sent.

**HAVE** — forms perfect tenses:
- HAVE + past participle (present perfect): Have you seen this film?
- HAD + past participle (past perfect): I had left before the meeting started.

---

## Section 236 — Have Been vs Have Gone

**HAVE BEEN** — went and returned (now back):
- He has been to Paris. (he went and is back)

**HAVE GONE** — went and is still there:
- He has gone to Paris. (he is in Paris now)

---

## Section 237 — Last / Next vs The Last / The Next

**Without THE** — before or after now (no comma):
- LAST / NEXT + day, week, month: I saw her last week. / We're going on holiday next month.

**With THE** — referring to a specific one in a series:
- THE LAST / THE NEXT + stop, time, day: This is the last day of the course. / The next train leaves at 10:15.

**Duration — FOR THE LAST / NEXT + time period:**
- I've lived here for the last 2 years.
- I'll be busy for the next few days.

---

## Section 238 — Present & Past Subjunctive

The subjunctive is a verb form used after certain expressions of demand, suggestion, importance, or unreality.

**PRESENT SUBJUNCTIVE** — used after verbs like *insist, suggest, recommend, demand*, and adjectives like *essential, important, vital*. The verb is in **base form** for all persons.

| Form | Example |
|---|---|
| base form | They insisted that he leave early. |
| not + base form | It is vital that he not be late. |
| be (for all persons) | It is essential that she be informed. |
| should + infinitive (British English) | It is essential that she should be informed. |

**PAST SUBJUNCTIVE / UNREAL PAST** — used after conditionals (if / wish / if only), *as if*, *would rather*, *it's time*. The verb is in past form to express an unreal present/future.

| Form | Example |
|---|---|
| were (for all persons) | I wish she were here. |
| past simple | I'd rather he stayed here. |
| past perfect | He looks as if he had been up all night. |

---

## Section 239 — Mixed Conditionals

Used to mix time references across the IF clause and the main clause. Two main types.

**Type A — Past condition → Present result:**
Structure: If + past perfect, … would + infinitive.
- If I had studied medicine, I would be a doctor now.
- If she hadn't missed the flight, she would be in Tokyo today.
- If we had bought that house in 2010, we would be much richer now.

**Type B — Present/permanent condition → Past result:**
Structure: If + past simple, … would have + past participle.
- If he were more responsible, he wouldn't have lost his job.
- If I didn't hate flying, I would have visited you last year.
- If she spoke French, she would have been hired for the Paris office.

**Rules:**
- The IF clause and the main clause refer to DIFFERENT time periods.
- Use only when the meaning truly mixes times — don't force it where a standard 2nd/3rd conditional fits.
- "If I were" is preferred over "If I was" in formal writing.

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ If I had studied more, I would have a better job now. (mixing tenses correctly, but learners often default to "would have had")
- ✅ If I had studied more, I would have a better job now. ← Type A done right
- ❌ If he were more careful, he wouldn't lose his keys last week. → ✅ If he were more careful, he wouldn't have lost his keys last week. (Type B)

---

## Section 240 — Modal Perfects (Regret, Criticism, Speculation)

Modal + have + past participle. Used to comment on past actions: regret, criticism, deduction.

**SHOULD HAVE / SHOULDN'T HAVE** — regret about own actions, criticism of others:
- I should have called you yesterday. (regret — I didn't)
- You shouldn't have spent so much on that bag. (criticism — you did)
- They should have warned us about the strike.

**COULD HAVE / COULDN'T HAVE** — past possibility that didn't happen; missed opportunity:
- You could have told me! (you didn't, and I'm upset)
- I could have been a contender. (but I wasn't)
- She couldn't have known — nobody told her.

**WOULD HAVE** — hypothetical past actions (often paired with a 3rd-conditional IF, but also standalone):
- I would have helped, but nobody asked.
- He would have apologized if he had been there.

**MIGHT HAVE / MAY HAVE** — past speculation (similar to "must have" but weaker):
- She might have forgotten — let me call her.
- They may have already left by the time we arrived.

**MUST HAVE / CAN'T HAVE** — strong past deduction (already covered in Section 076, included here for contrast):
- He must have missed the bus. / She can't have heard us.

**Rules:**
- The past participle never changes form across modals: should have GONE, could have GONE, must have GONE.
- In speech, "have" reduces to /ə/: "should've", "could've", "would've". Do NOT write "should of" — that's a common spelling error.
- For criticism, "should have" is softer than "ought to have"; both are correct.

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ I should of called. → ✅ I should have called.
- ❌ He should called earlier. → ✅ He should have called earlier.
- ❌ You could told me! → ✅ You could have told me!

---

## Section 241 — Gradable vs Non-Gradable Adjectives

Adjectives split into two groups by whether they can vary in degree. The intensifier you use depends on the type.

**GRADABLE adjectives** (can be more or less): cold, tired, interesting, expensive, big, good, hungry.
Use with: very, really, quite, fairly, rather, a bit, slightly, extremely.
- It's very cold today.
- She's quite tired.
- The film was really interesting.

**NON-GRADABLE adjectives** — already extreme, absolute, or classifying. Cannot vary.
- *Extremes:* freezing, exhausted, fascinating, enormous, terrible, delicious, starving.
- *Absolutes:* perfect, dead, unique, impossible, identical, essential.
- *Classifying:* electronic, digital, French, wooden, weekly.
Use with: absolutely, completely, totally, utterly, simply.
- It's absolutely freezing today. (NOT *very freezing*)
- She's utterly exhausted.
- The film was simply fascinating.

**REALLY** works with both groups — safe default if unsure.

**Common collocations to memorize:**

| Gradable | Non-gradable equivalent |
|---|---|
| very cold | absolutely freezing |
| very hot | absolutely boiling |
| very tired | completely exhausted |
| very hungry | absolutely starving |
| very surprised | absolutely amazed |
| very good | absolutely brilliant / perfect |
| very bad | absolutely terrible / awful |
| very big | absolutely enormous |
| very small | absolutely tiny |
| very angry | absolutely furious |

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ It's very freezing. → ✅ It's absolutely freezing.
- ❌ She's absolutely tired. → ✅ She's absolutely exhausted. / She's very tired.
- ❌ The view is very perfect. → ✅ The view is absolutely perfect.

## Section 242 — Inverted Conditionals (Formal)

Formal/literary alternative to standard conditionals. The IF is dropped and the auxiliary moves to the front. More common in writing, formal speech, and journalism.

**1st conditional inversion** — with "should" for unlikely but possible future events:
Structure: Should + subject + infinitive, … will/can/may + infinitive.
- Should you need any assistance, please call reception. (= If you should need…)
- Should the package arrive damaged, contact us immediately.

**2nd conditional inversion** — with "were" (formal/hypothetical present or future):
Structure: Were + subject + (to-infinitive / complement), … would + infinitive.
- Were I in your position, I would resign immediately. (= If I were…)
- Were the company to collapse, hundreds of jobs would be lost.

**3rd conditional inversion** — with "had" (hypothetical past):
Structure: Had + subject + past participle, … would have + past participle.
- Had I known about the meeting, I would have attended. (= If I had known…)
- Had it not been for your help, we would have failed.

**Rules:**
- Always formal register. Don't use in casual conversation.
- "Had it not been for…" and "Were it not for…" are fixed expressions meaning "without".
- The inverted form NEVER takes "if". "Had if I known" is wrong.

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ If had I known, I would have called. → ✅ Had I known, I would have called.
- ❌ Were I would be you… → ✅ Were I in your position… / If I were you…
- ❌ Should you would need help… → ✅ Should you need help…

---

## Section 243 — Past Perfect Continuous

Structure: had + been + verb-ing. Emphasizes the DURATION or ONGOING nature of an action that was in progress before another past moment.

**Positive:** I had been working all night when the power went out.
**Negative:** She hadn't been sleeping well before the trip.
**Question:** Had they been waiting long when you arrived?

**Main uses:**

1. **Duration up to a point in the past** (parallel to Present Perfect Continuous but anchored in the past):
   - By 2020, she had been teaching for thirty years.
   - When I finally got the job, I had been applying for months.

2. **Cause of a past state or result** — explains WHY something was the case at a past moment:
   - His eyes were red because he had been crying.
   - The streets were wet — it had been raining all night.
   - I was exhausted; I had been running for over an hour.

3. **Repeated/continued action interrupted by another past event:**
   - We had been driving for six hours when the car broke down.

**Past Perfect Simple vs Continuous:**
- Simple → completed action / result. "When I arrived, she had cooked dinner." (it was ready)
- Continuous → ongoing process / duration. "When I arrived, she had been cooking for an hour." (still going / just finished)

**Rules:**
- Use with stative verbs is rare — prefer Past Perfect Simple with be, know, have, believe, etc.
  - ❌ I had been knowing him for years. → ✅ I had known him for years.
- Time markers that pair well: for, since, all day/night, before, by the time, when.

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ When she called, I had work for two hours. → ✅ When she called, I had been working for two hours.
- ❌ He was tired because he had ran a marathon. → ✅ He was tired because he had been running / had run a marathon.

## Section 244 — Emphatic DO / DOES / DID

Used to add emphasis to an affirmative verb. Common in writing for contrast, contradiction, or insistence.

**Structure:** Subject + do/does/did + base form of the main verb (NOT a participle, NOT a third-person -s).

**Present (do / does):**
- I do believe you — I just need more evidence.
- She does work hard, despite what people say.
- They do care about quality.

**Past (did):**
- I did call you yesterday — check your phone.
- He did finish the report on time.
- We did warn them about the risks.

**Imperative (do):**
- Do come in — make yourself at home.
- Do let me know if you need anything.

**Common contexts in writing:**

1. **Contradicting a negative claim:**
   - Critics say the policy failed, but it did achieve its core goal.
   - "You never listen." — "I do listen, I just disagree."

2. **Conceding a point before pivoting (academic / argumentative writing):**
   - The data does suggest a correlation; however, correlation is not causation.
   - The author does raise valid concerns, although her conclusions overreach.

3. **Insistence / sincerity:**
   - I do appreciate everything you've done.

**Rules:**
- The emphasized verb stays in BASE form: "She does work hard" (NOT *does works*), "He did finish" (NOT *did finished*).
- Cannot combine with another auxiliary: ❌ "She does is working" → ✅ "She IS working" (stress the existing auxiliary instead).
- In speech, the emphasis is carried by stress on do/does/did.
- Overusing it sounds defensive. Reserve for genuine contrast or concession.

**Common mistakes:**
- ❌ She does works very hard. → ✅ She does work very hard.
- ❌ I did called him. → ✅ I did call him.
- ❌ He do believe it. → ✅ He does believe it.
